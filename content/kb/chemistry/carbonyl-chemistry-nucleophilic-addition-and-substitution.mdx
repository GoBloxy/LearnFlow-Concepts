# Carbonyl Chemistry: Nucleophilic Addition and Substitution

## Introduction
The carbonyl group ($\mathrm{C\!=\!O}$) is one of the most important functional groups in organic chemistry. It is found in a huge variety of compounds, from simple aldehydes and ketones to carboxylic acids, esters, amides, and acid chlorides. The polar nature of the carbonyl bond makes the carbon atom electrophilic, setting the stage for two fundamental reaction pathways: **nucleophilic addition** (for aldehydes and ketones) and **nucleophilic acyl substitution** (for carboxylic acid derivatives). Understanding these mechanisms is central to predicting reactivity, designing syntheses, and explaining the behavior of biological molecules.

## 1. Structure and Electronic Properties
A carbonyl group consists of a carbon‑oxygen double bond. The greater electronegativity of oxygen polarises the bond, giving the carbon a partial positive charge and the oxygen a partial negative charge:

$$\mathrm{R_2C^{\delta+}\!\!=\!O^{\delta-}}$$

This polarisation makes the carbonyl carbon strongly electrophilic and susceptible to attack by nucleophiles (Nu$^-$ or neutral nucleophiles with a labile proton). The oxygen can accept protons or coordinate to Lewis acids, which can further enhance the electrophilicity of the carbon.

In **carboxylic acid derivatives** ($\mathrm{R\!-\!C(\!=\!O)\!-\!Y}$), the group $\mathrm{Y}$ (e.g., $\mathrm{-Cl}$, $\mathrm{-OOCR}$, $\mathrm{-OR}$, $\mathrm{-NH_2}$) possesses a lone pair that can engage in resonance with the carbonyl $\pi$‑system (Figure 1). This donation raises the electron density on the carbonyl carbon, making it *less* electrophilic than in aldehydes or ketones. The extent of this resonance effect and the leaving ability of $\mathrm{Y^-}$ dictate the reactivity of the derivative.

## 2. Nucleophilic Addition to Aldehydes and Ketones
Aldehydes and ketones lack a good leaving group at the carbonyl carbon. Consequently, when a nucleophile attacks, the initially formed tetrahedral intermediate is not able to expel a stable leaving group. Instead, it simply gets protonated (if an anionic nucleophile was used) or undergoes further transformations (e.g., dehydration). This pathway is termed **nucleophilic addition**.

### 2.1 General Mechanism
1. **Nucleophilic attack:** The nucleophile donates an electron pair to the electrophilic carbonyl carbon. The $\pi$‑bond breaks, and both electrons move onto oxygen, generating a tetrahedral alkoxide intermediate.
   $$\mathrm{R_2C\!=\!O \;+\; Nu^- \;\longrightarrow\; R_2C(O^-)\!-\!Nu}$$
2. **Protonation:** In a separate step (often during work‑up), the alkoxide is protonated to give a neutral alcohol derivative.
   $$\mathrm{R_2C(O^-)\!-\!Nu \;+\; H^+ \;\longrightarrow\; R_2C(OH)\!-\!Nu}$$

When the nucleophile is neutral (e.g., water, alcohol, amine), the initial attack is followed by proton transfers to give the same net result.

### 2.2 Reactivity Trends
- **Aldehydes > Ketones:** Aldehydes have only one alkyl group donating electrons inductively, while ketones have two. The carbonyl carbon in an aldehyde is therefore more electrophilic. Sterically, the carbonyl of an aldehyde is less hindered, facilitating attack.
- **Formaldehyde** ($\mathrm{H_2C\!=\!O}$) is the most reactive carbonyl.
- Electron‑withdrawing substituents on the $\alpha$‑carbon increase reactivity; electron‑donating groups decrease it.

### 2.3 Key Examples

#### Hydration
Water adds reversibly to aldehydes and ketones to form **gem‑diols** (hydrates). The equilibrium usually favours the carbonyl compound, except for formaldehyde (formalin) and compounds with strongly electron‑withdrawing groups (e.g., chloral).
$$\mathrm{RCHO \;+\; H_2O \;\rightleftharpoons\; RCH(OH)_2}$$

#### Acetal Formation
Aldehydes and ketones react with two equivalents of an alcohol in the presence of an acid catalyst to yield **acetals** (ketals). The reaction is reversible; removing water drives it to completion.
$$\mathrm{RCHO \;+\; 2\,R'OH \;\xrightarrow{H^+}\; RCH(OR')_2 \;+\; H_2O}$$
Mechanism: hemiacetal intermediate → oxocarbenium ion → acetal. Acetals are used extensively as protecting groups for carbonyls.

#### Reactions with Ammonia Derivatives (Imines and Enamines)
- **Primary amines** react with aldehydes/ketones to give **imines** (Schiff bases). The addition forms a carbinolamine, which then dehydrates (often acid‑catalysed) to the imine.
  $$\mathrm{R_2C\!=\!O \;+\; R'NH_2 \;\longrightarrow\; R_2C\!=\!NR' \;+\; H_2O}$$
- **Secondary amines** give **enamines** when at least one $\alpha$‑hydrogen is present. After addition and dehydration, the iminium ion tautomerises to the enamine.
  $$\mathrm{R_2CH\!-\!C(\!=\!O)R' \;+\; R''_2NH \;\longrightarrow\; R_2C\!=\!C(R')\!-\!NR''_2 \;+\; H_2O}$$

#### Cyanohydrin Formation
Addition of cyanide ion to an aldehyde or ketone gives a cyanohydrin – a useful synthetic intermediate.
$$\mathrm{R_2C\!=\!O \;+\; HCN \;\leftrightharpoons\; R_2C(OH)CN}$$
The reaction is catalysed by base (generating CN$^-$). Cyanohydrins can be hydrolysed to $\alpha$‑hydroxy acids.

#### Hydride Reduction
Metal hydrides ($\mathrm{NaBH_4}$, $\mathrm{LiAlH_4}$) deliver a hydride ion ($\mathrm{H^-}$) to the carbonyl carbon. The resulting alkoxide is protonated upon work‑up to yield an alcohol.
$$\mathrm{R_2C\!=\!O \;\xrightarrow{1.\;HA, \; 2.\;H_3O^+}\; R_2CHOH}$$
$\mathrm{NaBH_4}$ reduces aldehydes and ketones selectively; $\mathrm{LiAlH_4}$ also reduces esters, acids, and amides.
