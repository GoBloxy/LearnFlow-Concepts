# Functional Groups and Nomenclature

## Introduction

Organic chemistry is built upon a relatively small number of fundamental structural units called **functional groups**. A functional group is an atom or a group of atoms within a molecule that exhibits a characteristic set of chemical reactions, largely independent of the rest of the molecule. By recognising the functional groups present, a chemist can predict the reactivity, physical properties, and spectroscopic behaviour of a compound. To communicate unambiguously about the millions of known organic compounds, the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) has developed a systematic nomenclature system. This system assigns a unique name to each structure based on a hierarchy of rules that prioritise the principal functional group, the longest carbon chain, and the position of substituents. In this article, we will explore the most common functional groups and learn how to apply IUPAC nomenclature to name organic molecules.

## 1. The Concept of a Functional Group

A carbon skeleton, whether linear, branched, or cyclic, can display remarkably different chemistry depending on the atoms or bonds attached to it. The **functional group** is the reactive centre of the molecule. For example, ethanol ($\mathrm{CH_3CH_2OH}$) and ethanoic acid ($\mathrm{CH_3COOH}$) both contain a two‑carbon chain, but the presence of the hydroxyl ($-OH$) group in the former and the carboxyl ($-COOH$) group in the latter gives them entirely different acid–base and redox properties. Molecules that contain the same functional group form a **homologous series**, in which physical properties change gradually with increasing chain length while chemical reactivity remains similar.

## 2. IUPAC Nomenclature – Systematic Framework

The IUPAC name of an organic compound conveys its complete structure. The general procedure for constructing a systematic name consists of the following steps:

1. **Identify the principal functional group** – the group with the highest priority according to the established order (see Section 4). This group will determine the **suffix** of the name.
2. **Select the parent chain** – the longest continuous carbon chain that contains the principal functional group. If two chains have the same length, choose the one with the greater number of substituents.
3. **Number the parent chain** – assign numbers (locants) to the carbon atoms so that the principal functional group receives the lowest possible number. If a choice remains, minimise the locants of double/triple bonds, then of substituents.
4. **Name and number the substituents** – groups attached to the parent chain. List them alphabetically (ignoring multiplying prefixes like di‑, tri‑, etc., except when part of a complex substituent name), each preceded by its locant.
5. **Assemble the name** – combine the locants, substituent prefixes, parent chain name (with unsaturation infixes), and the principal group suffix, separating numbers from letters with hyphens and numbers from numbers with commas.

## 3. Common Functional Groups and Their Nomenclature

### 3.1 Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons consist only of carbon and hydrogen. They are the simplest organic families and serve as the backbone for functionalised derivatives.  
- **Alkanes** (C–C single bonds): suffix **‑ane**. Example: $\mathrm{CH_3CH_2CH_3}$ → propane.  
- **Alkenes** (C=C double bond): suffix **‑ene**. The double bond requires a locant indicating its first carbon, e.g., $\mathrm{CH_2{=}CHCH_2CH_3}$ → but‑1‑ene.  
- **Alkynes** (C≡C triple bond): suffix **‑yne**. Example: $\mathrm{HC{\equiv}CCH_2CH_3}$ → but‑1‑yne.  
- **Aromatic rings**: the parent is **benzene**. A benzene ring as a substituent is called a **phenyl** group.

### 3.2 Alkyl Halides

General formula: $\mathrm{R{-}X}$ (X = F, Cl, Br, I). Named by prefix **fluoro‑, chloro‑, bromo‑, iodo‑** on the parent alkane.  
Example: $\mathrm{CH_3CHBrCH_3}$ → 2‑bromopropane.

### 3.3 Alcohols and Phenols

- **Alcohols** contain the hydroxyl group ($-OH$) attached to an $sp^3$ carbon. Suffix **‑ol**, position number before the parent name; if a higher priority group is present, the hydroxyl becomes a **hydroxy** prefix.  
  Example: $\mathrm{CH_3CH_2OH}$ → ethanol.  
- **Phenols** are hydroxy derivatives of benzene; the parent name is phenol, e.g., 4‑methylphenol.

### 3.4 Ethers

General structure $\mathrm{R{-}O{-}R'}$. In IUPAC, the smaller alkyl group together with the oxygen is treated as an **alkoxy** substituent ($\mathrm{RO{-}}$) on the larger alkane.  
Example: $\mathrm{CH_3OCH_2CH_3}$ → methoxyethane (common name: ethyl methyl ether).

### 3.5 Aldehydes and Ketones

Both contain the carbonyl group ($\mathrm{C{=}O}$).  
- **Aldehydes**: at least one hydrogen attached to carbonyl carbon ($\mathrm{{-}CHO}$). Suffix **‑al**; the carbonyl carbon is always C‑1 and does not require a locant.  
  Example: $\mathrm{CH_3CH_2CHO}$ → propanal.  
- **Ketones**: carbonyl carbon bonded to two other carbons. Suffix **‑one**, with a locant for the carbonyl group unless it is unambiguous.  
  Example: $\mathrm{CH_3COCH_2CH_3}$ → butan‑2‑one.

### 3.6 Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives

- **Carboxylic acids** ($-COOH$): suffix **‑oic acid**; the carboxyl carbon is always C‑1. Example: $\mathrm{CH_3COOH}$ → ethanoic acid.  
- **Esters** ($-COOR$): the alcohol part becomes an alkyl group and the acid part ends in **‑oate**. Example: $\mathrm{CH_3COOCH_2CH_3}$ → ethyl ethanoate.  
- **Amides** ($-CONH_2$): suffix **‑amide**. Example: $\mathrm{CH_3CONH_2}$ → ethanamide.  
- **Nitriles** ($-C{\equiv}N$): suffix **‑nitrile**; the nitrile carbon is part of the parent chain. Example: $\mathrm{CH_3CN}$ → ethanenitrile.

### 3.7 Amines

Amines contain the amino group ($-NH_2$, $-NHR$, $-NR_2$). The suffix is **‑amine** (e.g., ethanamine). When a higher priority group is present, the prefix **amino‑** is used.  
Example: $\mathrm{CH_3CH_2NH_2}$ → ethanamine.

### 3.8 Other Significant Groups

| Class | Functional Group | Suffix | Prefix (if lower priority) |
|-------|------------------|--------|---------------------------|
| Thiol | $\mathrm{{-}SH}$ | **‑thiol** | mercapto‑ (commonly sulfanyl‑) |
| Sulfonic acid | $\mathrm{{-}SO_3H}$ | **‑sulfonic acid** | sulfo‑ |
| Nitro | $\mathrm{{-}NO_2}$ | — | **nitro‑** (always prefix) |
| Alkene | $\mathrm{C{=}C}$ | **‑ene** | alkenyl‑ |
| Alkyne | $\mathrm{C{\equiv}C}$ | **‑yne** | alkynyl‑ |

*Note*: Suffixes are used only for the principal group; all other groups appear as prefixes.

## 4. Priority Order of Functional Groups

When a molecule contains more than one functional group, a strict hierarchy decides which group is named as the suffix. The following table lists common groups in decreasing priority (highest first):

| Priority | Functional Group (formula) | Suffix | Prefix |
|----------|----------------------------|--------|--------|
| 1 (highest) | Carboxylic acid ($-COOH$) | **‑oic acid** | carboxy‑ |
| 2 | Ester ($-COOR$) | **‑oate** | alkoxycarbonyl‑ |
| 3 | Amide ($-CONH_2$) | **‑amide** | carbamoyl‑ |
| 4 | Nitrile ($-C{\equiv}N$) | **‑nitrile** | cyano‑ |
| 5 | Aldehyde ($-CHO$) | **‑al** | formyl‑ (or oxo‑) |
| 6 | Ketone ($>C{=}O$) | **‑one** | oxo‑ |
| 7 | Alcohol ($-OH$) | **‑ol** | hydroxy‑ |
| 8 | Amine ($-NH_2$) | **‑amine** | amino‑ |
| 9 | Alkene ($C{=}C$) | **‑ene** | — |
| 10 | Alkyne ($C{\equiv}C$) | **‑yne** | — |
| 11 | Alkane ($C{-}C$, $C{-}H$) | **‑ane** | — |
| (lower) | Ether ($-OR$) | — | alkoxy‑ |
| (lower) | Halide ($-X$) | — | fluoro‑, chloro‑, etc. |
| (lower) | Nitro ($-NO_2$) | — | nitro‑ |

In naming, the principal group provides the suffix; all lower-priority groups (and substituents) are listed as prefixes in alphabetical order.

## 5. Naming Polyfunctional Compounds – Worked Examples

### Example 1: 4‑Hydroxybutanoic acid
Structure: $\mathrm{HOCH_2CH_2CH_2COOH}$  
- Longest chain with COOH: 4 carbons → butane backbone.  
- Principal group: carboxylic acid → suffix **‑oic acid** → butanoic acid.  
- OH group (lower priority) → prefix **hydroxy** at C‑4.  
Numbering: COOH carbon = C‑1, the OH is on C‑4.  
Full name: **4‑hydroxybutanoic acid**.

### Example 2: 3‑Methylbut‑2‑enal
Structure: $\mathrm{(CH_3)_2C{=}CHCHO}$  
- Chain with aldehyde and double bond: 4 carbons → but‑ → suffix **‑al**, infix **‑en‑** for double bond at C‑2.  
- Aldehyde C is C‑1, double bond between C‑2 and C‑3 → but‑2‑enal.  
- Methyl substituent at C‑3 → 3‑methyl.  
Full name: **3‑methylbut‑2‑enal**.

### Example 3: 2‑Aminocyclohexanone
Structure: cyclohexane ring with a ketone (C=O) and an amino group.  
- Parent ring: cyclohexane.  
- Principal group: ketone (higher than amine) → suffix **‑one**. Position of ketone is C‑1 (so we give it the number 1, though it is often omitted because the ring numbering starts at the carbonyl).  
- Amino group at C‑2
