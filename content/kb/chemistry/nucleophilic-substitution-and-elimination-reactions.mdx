# Nucleophilic Substitution and Elimination Reactions

## Introduction

Nucleophilic substitution and elimination are two of the most fundamental reaction classes in organic chemistry. Both involve the interaction of a nucleophile (or base) with an electrophilic carbon atom that bears a suitable leaving group. The nature of the substrate, the reagent, the solvent, and the temperature determine whether substitution (replacement of the leaving group by the nucleophile) or elimination (formation of a π-bond with loss of the leaving group and an adjacent proton) predominates. This article explores the four cornerstone mechanisms—S$_N$2, S$_N$1, E2, and E1—and the factors that govern their competition.

## Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

Nucleophilic substitution involves the displacement of a leaving group (LG) by a nucleophile (Nu). Two limiting mechanisms are recognized:

### The S$_N$2 Mechanism

S$_N$2 stands for **S**ubstitution, **N**ucleophilic, **bimolecular**. It is a concerted process in which bond formation and bond breaking occur simultaneously through a single transition state.

**Key features:**

- **Rate law:** $ \text{Rate} = k[\text{substrate}][\text{Nu}^-] $  
  The reaction is second order overall.
- **Stereochemistry:** Inversion of configuration at the carbon centre (Walden inversion). If the substrate is chiral, the product has the opposite absolute configuration.
- **Substrate reactivity:**  
  Methyl > primary > secondary >> tertiary (tertiary substrates are essentially unreactive in S$_N$2 due to extreme steric hindrance).
- **Solvent effects:** Polar aprotic solvents (e.g., acetone, DMSO, DMF) accelerate the reaction by solvating the cation but leaving the nucleophile highly reactive.
- **Nucleophile:** Strong, negatively charged nucleophiles favor S$_N$2; neutral nucleophiles (e.g., water, alcohols) usually react much more slowly.

**Example:**

Reaction of sodium hydroxide with methyl bromide:

$$\text{CH}_3\text{Br} + \text{OH}^- \xrightarrow{\text{acetone}} \text{CH}_3\text{OH} + \text{Br}^-$$

The hydroxide ion attacks the backside of the C–Br σ* orbital, leading to a transition state with a trigonal bipyramidal geometry. Inversion of the tetrahedron yields methanol.

---

### The S$_N$1 Mechanism

S$_N$1 stands for **S**ubstitution, **N**ucleophilic, **unimolecular**. It proceeds via a two‑step pathway: slow, rate‑determining ionization to a carbocation followed by rapid nucleophilic capture.

**Key features:**

- **Rate law:** $ \text{Rate} = k[\text{substrate}] $  
  The rate depends only on the concentration of the alkyl halide (or equivalent).
- **Stereochemistry:** The planar carbocation intermediate can be attacked from either face, leading to racemization (partial or total loss of optical activity).
- **Substrate reactivity:**  
  Tertiary > secondary > primary (methyl and primary substrates rarely proceed via S$_N$1 because of the instability of the corresponding carbocations).  
  Benzyl and allyl substrates also react well due to resonance-stabilized carbocations.
- **Solvent effects:** Polar protic solvents (water, alcohols, carboxylic acids) stabilize the carbocation and the leaving group by solvation, thereby facilitating ionization.
- **Rearrangements:** Because a carbocation is formed, hydride or alkyl shifts can occur to give more stable carbocations, leading to unexpected products.

**Example:**

Hydrolysis of *tert*-butyl bromide:

$$\text{(CH}_3\text{)}_3\text{C-Br} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_3\text{C-OH} + \text{HBr}$$

Step 1 (slow): $(CH_3)_3C-Br \rightarrow (CH_3)_3C^+ + Br^-$  
Step 2 (fast): $(CH_3)_3C^+ + H_2O \rightarrow (CH_3)_3C-OH_2^+ \rightarrow (CH_3)_3C-OH + H^+$

## Elimination Reactions

Elimination reactions form carbon–carbon π-bonds by removing a leaving group and a β-proton. The two main mechanisms parallel those of substitution.

### The E2 Mechanism

**E2** stands for **E**limination, **bimolecular**. It is a concerted anti‑elimination in which a base abstracts a β‑proton at the same time that the leaving group departs.

**Key features:**

- **Rate law:** $ \text{Rate} = k[\text{substrate}][\text{base}] $  
- **Geometry:** The proton and leaving group must be anti‑periplanar (dihedral angle ≈ 180°) in the transition state, which dictates the stereochemistry of the resulting alkene.
- **Substrate reactivity:**  
  Tertiary > secondary > primary (but even methyl halides can undergo E2 with a very strong base; however, methyl substrates lack a β‑proton and cannot eliminate).
- **Base strength:** Strong bases (e.g., $\text{OH}^-$, $\text{OR}^-$, $\text{NH}_2^-$) favor E2.
- **Regioselectivity:**  
  - **Zaitsev’s rule:** With small bases (e.g., $\text{OH}^-$ in ethanol), the more highly substituted, thus more stable alkene predominates.  
  - **Hofmann rule:** Bulky bases (e.g., potassium *tert*-butoxide) give the less substituted alkene because steric hindrance favors abstraction of the most accessible proton.
- **Solvents:** Often polar protic or aprotic solvents; the base concentration and temperature are critical.

**Example:**

Dehydrohalogenation of 2‑bromobutane with ethanolic KOH:

$$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{CHBrCH}_3 + \text{KOH} \xrightarrow{\text{EtOH}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH=CHCH}_3\ (\text{Zaitsev}) + \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{CH=CH}_2\ (\text{Hofmann})$$

The major product is *trans*-2‑butene (Zaitsev), with small amounts of 1‑butene.

---

### The E1 Mechanism

**E1** stands for **E**limination, **unimolecular**. It shares the same first step as S$_N$1: slow ionization to a carbocation, followed by fast loss of a β‑proton to a weak base (often the solvent).

**Key features:**

- **Rate law:** $ \text{Rate} = k[\text{substrate}] $  
- **Substrate:** Tertiary substrates (and those forming resonance‑stabilized carbocations) react efficiently.
- **Regioselectivity:** Strong preference for Zaitsev product because the carbocation intermediate loses the proton that leads to the most stable alkene.
- **Stereochemistry:** No strict geometric requirement because the carbocation is planar; E/Z mixtures are common.
- **Competition:** E1 always competes with S$_N$1. Typical S$_N$1/E1 mixtures are obtained when the nucleophile is also a weak base (e.g., water or alcohol under neutral or acidic conditions).
- **Rearrangements:** As with S$_N$1, carbocation rearrangements can occur.

**Example:**

Dehydration of *tert*-butyl alcohol in acidic medium:

$$\text{(CH}_3\text{)}_3\text{C-OH} \xrightarrow{\text{H}^+,\ \Delta} \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_2\text{C=CH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

The reaction proceeds via protonation of the –OH, loss of water to form the *tert*-butyl cation, and deprotonation to yield isobutylene.

## Competition Between Substitution and Elimination

In many synthetic situations, substitution and elimination compete. The outcome is governed by several interrelated factors:

| Factor | Favours S$_N$2 | Favours E2 | Favours S$_N$1/E1 |
|--------|----------------|------------|-------------------|
| **Substrate** | Methyl, 1° > 2° | 3° > 2° > 1° (works for 2°, 1° as well) | 3° > 2° (carbocation stability) |
| **Nucleophile / Base** | Good nucleophile, weak base (e.g., I$^-$, Br$^-$, RS$^-$, CN$^-$) | Strong base (e.g., OH$^-$, OR$^-$, NH$_2^-$) | Weak nucleophile / weak base (e.g., H$_2$O, ROH) |
| **Solvent** | Polar aprotic | Polar protic or aprotic possible | Polar protic (stabilizes ions) |
| **Temperature** | Lower temperature favours substitution (kinetic control) | Higher temperature favours elimination (greater entropy) | Higher temperature favours elimination |
| **Steric hindrance** | Disfavoured by bulky substrate and bulky nucleophile | Bulky base can direct regiochemistry (Hofmann) | Not a major factor for S$_N$1/E1 |

**Practical guidelines:**

- **1° alkyl halides:** S$_N$2 dominates with good nucleophiles (e.g., NaCN, NaI). If a strong, hindered base is used, E2 can compete (e.g., *tert*-BuO$^-$ gives mainly elimination). S$_N$1/E1 are negligible.
- **2° alkyl halides:** Strong nucleophiles that are weak bases (e.g., Br$^-$, I$^-$, CN$^-$) promote S$_N$2. Strong bases (e.g., EtO$^-$) promote E2. In neutral or acidic solutions, S$_N$1/E1 mixtures can form, but generally E2 is faster with strong base.
- **3° alkyl halides:** S$_N$2 is impossible. With strong bases, E2 is rapid. With weak bases/nucleophiles (e.g., H$_2$O, EtOH), S$_N$1/E1 mixtures are obtained; the ratio depends on the nucleophile’s strength and temperature. E1 is favoured at higher temperatures.
- **Temperature:** Because elimination reactions often produce more molecules (entropy increase), they are favoured by heat. For example, heating a 2° alkyl halide with OH$^-$ increases the E2:S$_N$2 ratio.

**Example – competition:**

Reaction of 2‑bromopropane with sodium ethoxide:

$$\text{CH}_3\text{CHBrCH}_3 + \text{NaOEt} \xrightarrow{\text{EtOH}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}=\text{CH}_2\ (\text{E2}) + \text{CH}_3\text{CH(OEt)CH}_3\ (\text{S}_N2)$$

At room temperature, both products are formed; the ratio is controlled by the base strength and temperature. Using a stronger, bulkier base (e.g., potassium *tert*-butoxide) suppresses substitution and gives almost exclusively propene.

## Summary

- Nucleophilic substitution and elimination are controlled by the same types of reagents and substrates but lead to different outcomes.
- **S$_N$2** is a concerted, stereospecific substitution favoured by strong nucleophiles, aprotic solvents, and unhindered substrates.
- **S$_N$1** proceeds via a carbocation, is favoured by tertiary substrates and polar protic solvents, and leads to racemization and possible rearrangements.
- **E2** is a concerted elimination requiring an anti‑periplanar geometry; strong bases and higher temperatures promote it. Regioselectivity follows Zaitsev’s rule unless a bulky base enforces Hofmann elimination.
- **E1** shares the carbocation intermediate with S$_N$1, so the two always compete; E1 selectivity is enhanced by heat.
- The choice of mechanism is dictated by:
  - Substrate structure (steric hindrance, carbocation stability)
  - Reagent character (nucleophilicity vs basicity, steric bulk)
  - Solvent (protic vs aprotic)
  - Temperature (lower favours substitution, higher favours elimination)
- Mastery of these principles allows chemists to design reactions that selectively produce either substitution or elimination products.
