# Addition Reactions of Alkenes and Alkynes

## Introduction

Alkenes (olefins) and alkynes (acetylenes) are hydrocarbons containing carbon–carbon multiple bonds: alkenes possess a double bond ($\ce{C=C}$), while alkynes contain a triple bond ($\ce{C#C}$). The $\pi$ electrons of these unsaturated systems are relatively loosely held and exposed, making them nucleophilic. Consequently, the most characteristic reaction of alkenes and alkynes is **electrophilic addition**. In an addition reaction, the $\pi$ bond is broken, and two new $\sigma$ bonds are formed as atoms or groups add across the unsaturated linkage.

Addition reactions are central to organic synthesis because they permit the functionalization of simple hydrocarbons, converting them into alcohols, alkyl halides, alkanes, and many other valuable products. Understanding the mechanistic patterns, regioselectivity, and stereochemistry of these reactions is essential for predicting product structures and designing synthetic routes.

This article explores the major addition reactions of alkenes and alkynes, with emphasis on mechanistic principles, including electrophilic addition, Markovnikov’s rule, stereochemical outcomes, and the distinctive features of alkyne additions such as double addition and tautomerism.

## Core Concepts: Addition Reactions of Alkenes

### 1. Electrophilic Addition Mechanism

The generic mechanism for electrophilic addition to an alkene involves two steps:

1. **Attack of the $\pi$ bond on the electrophile** – The electron-rich $\pi$ system donates electrons to an electrophile ($\ce{E+}$), generating a carbocation intermediate (or a bridged ion) and a new $\sigma$ bond.
2. **Capture of the carbocation by a nucleophile** – The carbocation reacts with a nucleophile ($\ce{Nu-}$) to form the final addition product.

$$\ce{>C=C< + E-Nu ->[\text{step 1}] >C^+-C(-E)< + Nu- ->[\text{step 2}] >C(Nu)-C(E)<}$$

The overall transformation is the addition of $\ce{E–Nu}$ across the double bond.

### 2. Regioselectivity: Markovnikov’s Rule

When an unsymmetrical alkene reacts with an unsymmetrical reagent ($\ce{HX, H2O, etc.}$), two constitutional isomers may be possible. **Markovnikov’s rule** states that the hydrogen (or electropositive part) adds to the less substituted carbon of the double bond, so as to form the most stable carbocation intermediate. In modern terms, the electrophile adds to the carbon that yields the more stable carbocation.

**Example:** Addition of $\ce{HBr}$ to propene.

$$\ce{CH3-CH=CH2 + HBr -> CH3-CHBr-CH3 (major)}$$
$$\ce{ -> CH3-CH2-CH2Br (minor)}$$

The secondary carbocation $\ce{(CH3-CH^+-CH3)}$ is more stable than the primary one, leading to 2-bromopropane as the major product.

**Anti-Markovnikov addition** occurs when radicals are involved (e.g., $\ce{HBr}$ with peroxides) or with hydroboration–oxidation, where the boron atom ends up at the less substituted carbon after oxidation.

### 3. Stereochemistry of Addition

Addition reactions to alkenes can occur with distinct stereochemical outcomes:

- **Syn addition:** Both new groups add to the same face of the double bond.
- **Anti addition:** The groups add to opposite faces.

The stereochemistry is dictated by the reaction mechanism. Some key examples:
- Catalytic hydrogenation and hydroboration are typically syn additions.
- Halogenation ($\ce{Br2, Cl2}$) follows an anti addition pathway via a cyclic halonium ion.
- Addition of $\ce{HX}$ in non-polar solvents often yields mixtures of syn and anti products because the intermediate carbocation is planar and can be attacked from either side.

When chiral centers are generated from a symmetrical alkene, a racemic mixture is obtained. With chiral reactants or catalysts, enantioselective addition is possible.

### 4. Major Addition Reactions of Alkenes

#### 4.1 Catalytic Hydrogenation

Alkenes react with hydrogen gas in the presence of a metal catalyst (Pt, Pd, Ni) to form alkanes. The addition is syn stereospecific; both hydrogen atoms are delivered to the same face of the alkene.

$$\ce{R2C=CR2 + H2 ->[Pt] R2CH-CHR2}$$

The reaction is exothermic and occurs on the metal surface. Hydrogenation is used for reducing unsaturated fats, synthesizing cycloalkanes, and evaluating alkene stability via heats of hydrogenation.

#### 4.2 Electrophilic Addition of Hydrogen Halides ($\ce{HX}$)

Hydrogen halides ($\ce{HCl, HBr, HI}$) add to alkenes to give alkyl halides. The reaction proceeds via protonation to form the most stable carbocation, followed by halide attack. Markovnikov orientation is observed unless peroxides are present with $\ce{HBr}$ (radical mechanism, anti-Markovnikov).

**Mechanism (Markovnikov $\ce{HBr}$ addition to 2-methylpropene):**
1. $\ce{(CH3)2C=CH2 + HBr -> (CH3)3C^+ + Br-}$
2. $\ce{(CH3)3C^+ + Br- -> (CH3)3CBr}$

#### 4.3 Acid-Catalyzed Hydration

Water adds to alkenes in the presence of a strong acid catalyst (e.g., $\ce{H2SO4}$) to yield alcohols. The reaction is Markovnikov-selective.

$$\ce{R2C=CH2 + H2O ->[H+] R2C(OH)-CH3}$$

The mechanism parallels $\ce{HX}$ addition: protonation gives the more stable carbocation, which is captured by water, then deprotonated. Rearrangements are common with carbocation intermediates.

#### 4.4 Halogenation ($\ce{X2}$)

Addition of bromine or chlorine to alkenes yields vicinal dihalides. The reaction proceeds with **anti addition** via a three-membered halonium ion intermediate.

$$\ce{R2C=CR2 + Br2 -> R2CBr-CBrR2}$$

**Mechanism:**
1. $\pi$ bond attacks $\ce{Br2}$, displacing bromide and forming a bromonium ion.
2. Bromide opens the bromonium ion from the backside (anti).

The disappearance of the characteristic red-brown color of bromine is a standard test for unsaturation.

#### 4.5 Halohydrin Formation

When halogenation is conducted in water, the nucleophile that attacks the halonium ion is water, leading to a halohydrin (a $\beta$-halo alcohol). The halogen adds to the less substituted carbon because the halonium ion opening is governed by partial carbocation character.

$$\ce{RCH=CH2 + Br2 + H2O -> RCH(OH)-CH2Br + HBr}$$

#### 4.6 Hydroboration–Oxidation

Borane ($\ce{BH3}$) or alkylboranes add to alkenes in a syn fashion, placing boron at the less hindered carbon (anti-Markovnikov stereochemistry). Subsequent oxidation with hydrogen peroxide in base replaces the boron with a hydroxyl group, yielding an alcohol with overall anti-Markovnikov regiochemistry and syn stereochemistry.

$$\ce{RCH=CH2 ->[1. BH3, THF][2. H2O2, NaOH] RCH2-CH2OH}$$

This reaction is particularly valuable because it provides the regiochemical complement to acid-catalyzed hydration and does not involve carbocation rearrangements.

#### 4.7 Epoxidation and Further Additions

Alkenes react with peroxyacids (e.g., mCPBA) to form epoxides. This is a syn addition of an oxygen atom. Epoxides can be ring-opened by nucleophiles under acidic or basic conditions, leading to anti 1,2-difunctionalized products.

#### 4.8 Carbene and Cyclopropanation

Alkenes react with carbenes (or carbenoids) to give cyclopropane derivatives. The addition is stereospecific: the stereochemistry of the alkene is retained in the cyclopropane (cis-alkene gives cis-substituted cyclopropane).

## Core Concepts: Addition Reactions of Alkynes

Alkynes exhibit addition chemistry similar to alkenes, but with important differences arising from the presence of two $\pi$ bonds. Most addition reactions can occur twice: the triple bond can first be converted to a double bond (vinyl derivative) and then to a single bond. However, because a double bond is more reactive than a triple bond toward electrophilic addition, careful control is often required to stop at the alkene stage.

### 1. Electrophilic Addition to Alkynes

Alkynes are less reactive than alkenes toward typical electrophiles because the $sp$-hybridized carbons hold the $\pi$ electrons more tightly. Nevertheless, they undergo addition with strong electrophiles. The vinyl carbocation intermediate is generally less stable than an alkyl carbocation, but the reaction still proceeds with Markovnikov orientation when applicable.

### 2. Major Addition Reactions of Alkynes

#### 2.1 Catalytic Hydrogenation

Alkynes can be hydrogenated to alkanes with standard catalysts ($\ce{Pt, Pd}$). Partial hydrogenation to cis-alkenes is achieved using poisoned catalysts (Lindlar’s catalyst: $\ce{Pd/CaCO3}$ with lead acetate and quinoline) which permit syn addition and stop at the alkene. Additionally, dissolving metal reduction ($\ce{Na}$ in liquid $\ce{NH3}$) gives the trans-alkene via anti addition of hydrogen.

$$\ce{RC#CR' + 2H2 ->[Pd] RCH2-CH2R'}$$

$$\ce{RC#CR' + H2 ->[Lindlar] cis-RCH=CHR'}$$

$$\ce{RC#CR' + Na/NH3 -> trans-RCH=CHR'}$$

#### 2.2 Addition of Hydrogen Halides ($\ce{HX}$)

One or two equivalents of $\ce{HX}$ can be added. The first addition yields a vinyl halide, typically with Markovnikov orientation. The second addition gives a geminal dihalide, again following Markovnikov’s rule (the halogen ends up on the more substituted carbon). With peroxides, $\ce{HBr}$ adds anti-Markovnikov to give the vinyl bromide with opposite regiochemistry.

$$\ce{RC#CH + HBr -> R-CBr=CH2 ->[HBr] R-CBr2-CH3}$$

#### 2.3 Hydration of Alkynes

In the presence of mercury(II) catalysts (e.g., $\ce{HgSO4}$ in aqueous $\ce{H2SO4}$), water adds to alkynes. The initial addition yields an **enol**, which immediately tautomerizes to the more stable carbonyl compound. For terminal alkynes, the product is a methyl ketone; for internal alkynes, a mixture of ketones may result unless symmetrical.

$$\ce{RC#CH + H2O ->[HgSO4, H2SO4] [R-C(OH)=CH2] -> R-CO-CH3}$$

The enol–keto tautomerism is a hallmark of alkyne hydration. Hydroboration–oxidation of terminal alkynes also leads to aldehydes (anti-Markovnikov hydration) via a similar enol intermediate.

$$\ce{RC#CH ->[1. BH3, THF][2. H2O2, NaOH] [R-CH=CH-OH] -> R-CH2-CHO}$$

#### 2.4 Halogenation

Alkynes add one or two equivalents of halogen. The reaction with bromine or chlorine yields a tetrahaloalkane if excess halogen is used. The addition is anti, proceeding through a bridged halonium ion in the first step to give a trans-dihaloalkene, which can then add a second halogen molecule.

$$\ce{RC#CR' + 2Br2 -> R-CBr2-CBr2-R'}$$

#### 2.5 Hydrohalogenation and Other Additions

The principles extend to additions of other electrophilic species. Conjugate addition and nucleophilic additions are also possible for alkynes conjugated with electron-withdrawing groups, but these are beyond the scope of this article.

## Examples and Problem Solving

### Example 1: Predicting Regiochemistry
Predict the major product when 1-methylcyclohexene is treated with $\ce{HBr}$.

**Solution:** Protonation gives a tertiary carbocation at the 1-position (more substituted carbon of the double bond). Bromide attack yields 1-bromo-1-methylcyclohexane (Markovnikov addition). No rearrangement occurs because the carbocation is already tertiary.

### Example 2: Stereochemical Outcome
What is the stereochemistry of the product when cis-2-butene is treated with $\ce{Br2}$ in $\ce{CCl4}$?

**Solution:** The addition is anti. Starting from cis-2-butene, the two bromines add from opposite faces, leading to a racemic mixture of (2R,3S)-2,3-dibromobutane and (2S,3R)-2,3-dibromobutane, which are identical because the molecule is meso? Wait: cis-2-butene gives the anti addition product: both bromines add trans to each other, forming the (2R,3S) isomer which has an internal plane of symmetry, so it’s a meso compound. So the product is the meso-2,3-dibromobutane (only one stereoisomer). In contrast, trans-2-butene with $\ce{Br2}$ gives a racemic mixture of (2R,3R) and (2S,3S) enantiomers.

### Example 3: Alkyne Hydration
Show how to convert 1-butyne to 2-butanone.

**Solution:** Hydration of 1-butyne ($\ce{CH3CH2C#CH}$) using $\ce{HgSO4}$ in $\ce{H2SO4}$/ $\ce{H2O}$ yields the enol $\ce{CH3CH2C(OH)=CH2}$, which tautomerizes to 2-butanone ($\ce{CH3CH2COCH3}$).

### Example 4: Synthetic Strategy
Propose a synthesis of trans-3-hexene from 3-hexyne.

**Solution:** Use dissolving metal reduction: $\ce{Na}$ in liquid $\ce{NH3}$ reduces the alkyne to the trans-alkene with anti addition of hydrogen.

## Summary

- Addition reactions are the principal reaction manifold for alkenes and alkynes, exploiting the reactivity of $\pi$ bonds toward electrophiles.
- The general mechanism for alkenes involves formation of a carbocation (or cyclic intermediate) and subsequent nucleophilic capture.
- Regioselectivity is typically governed by Markovnikov’s rule, which favors the more stable carbocation, unless radical or concerted pathways (hydroboration) impose anti-Markovnikov orientation.
- Stereochemistry can be syn or anti, depending on the reaction: hydrogenation and hydroboration are syn; halogenation is anti; $\ce{HX}$ addition is often non-stereoselective.
- Alkynes undergo analogous additions but can react twice; partial addition allows access to alkenes with defined stereochemistry (Lindlar for cis, dissolving metal for trans).
- Hydration of alkynes proceeds through enols that tautomerize to carbonyl compounds, a key method for preparing aldehydes and ketones.
- Mastery of these reactions enables the rational synthesis of a wide array of organic compounds from simple unsaturated starting materials.
