# Glycolysis

## 1. Introduction

Glycolysis (from the Greek *glykys*, “sweet,” and *lysis*, “splitting”) is a nearly universal metabolic pathway that converts one molecule of glucose (a six‑carbon sugar) into two molecules of pyruvate (a three‑carbon compound). It occurs in the cytosol of virtually all living cells and does not require oxygen, making it an anaerobic process. Glycolysis serves as the first stage of cellular respiration, providing both a modest amount of ATP and key intermediates for other metabolic pathways. For many organisms, it is the sole source of metabolic energy.

The pathway is a sequence of ten enzyme‑catalysed reactions. The overall balanced equation is:

$$
\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 + 2\,\text{NAD}^+ + 2\,\text{ADP} + 2\,\text{P}_i \rightarrow 2\,\text{C}_3\text{H}_4\text{O}_3 + 2\,\text{NADH} + 2\,\text{H}^+ + 2\,\text{ATP} + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

In this article we explore the two phases of glycolysis, examine each enzymatic step, discuss the energetics and regulation of the pathway, and illustrate its central role with concrete examples.

## 2. Overview of Glycolysis

Glycolysis can be divided into two distinct phases:

1. **Energy investment phase (reactions 1–5)**  
   Two molecules of ATP are consumed to phosphorylate glucose and its early intermediates, “priming” the sugar for subsequent cleavage. At the end of this phase, the six‑carbon skeleton is split into two three‑carbon molecules: glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate (G3P) and dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP). DHAP is rapidly isomerised to a second molecule of G3P, so the remainder of the pathway effectively proceeds with two identical triose phosphates.

2. **Energy payoff phase (reactions 6–10)**  
   The two triose phosphates are oxidised and rearranged, generating four ATP molecules through substrate‑level phosphorylation and two molecules of NADH. The net gain is two ATP per glucose (four produced minus two invested) and two NADH.

All intermediates are phosphorylated, which serves two purposes: the charged phosphate groups prevent the intermediates from diffusing out of the cell, and they participate directly in the chemistry of substrate‑level phosphorylation.

## 3. The Reactions of Glycolysis

Below, each step is described with the enzyme, the reaction, and salient thermodynamic or regulatory features.

### 3.1 Energy Investment Phase (Steps 1–5)

**Step 1: Phosphorylation of glucose**  
*Enzyme: hexokinase (or glucokinase in liver)*

$$
\text{Glucose} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{Mg}^{2+}} \text{Glucose‑6‑phosphate} + \text{ADP} + \text{H}^+
$$

Hexokinase transfers a phosphoryl group from ATP to the C‑6 hydroxyl of glucose. This irreversible reaction traps glucose inside the cell and commits it to metabolism. It is inhibited by its product, glucose‑6‑phosphate. The liver isoenzyme glucokinase has a higher $K_m$ for glucose and is not inhibited by glucose‑6‑phosphate, allowing the liver to phosphorylate glucose even when blood glucose is high.

**Step 2: Isomerisation of glucose‑6‑phosphate**  
*Enzyme: phosphoglucose isomerase*

$$
\text{Glucose‑6‑phosphate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Fructose‑6‑phosphate}
$$

The aldose sugar is converted to the ketose fructose‑6‑phosphate. This isomerisation, which is readily reversible, moves the carbonyl closer to C‑1 and prepares the molecule for a second phosphorylation.

**Step 3: Second phosphorylation**  
*Enzyme: phosphofructokinase‑1 (PFK‑1)*

$$
\text{Fructose‑6‑phosphate} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{Mg}^{2+}} \text{Fructose‑1,6‑bisphosphate} + \text{ADP} + \text{H}^+
$$

PFK‑1 catalyses the rate‑limiting, irreversible step of glycolysis. It is allosterically inhibited by ATP and citrate (signals of high energy) and activated by AMP, ADP, and fructose‑2,6‑bisphosphate. This elaborate regulation ensures that flux through glycolysis matches the cell’s energy demands.

**Step 4: Cleavage of the hexose**  
*Enzyme: aldolase*

$$
\text{Fructose‑1,6‑bisphosphate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Dihydroxyacetone phosphate} + \text{Glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate}
$$

Aldolase carries out a reversible aldol cleavage, producing one ketose (DHAP) and one aldose (G3P) triose phosphate. Under standard conditions the equilibrium favours the hexose, but in the cell the products are rapidly consumed, pulling the reaction forward.

**Step 5: Triose phosphate isomerisation**  
*Enzyme: triose phosphate isomerase (TIM)*

$$
\text{Dihydroxyacetone phosphate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate}
$$

DHAP is isomerised to a second molecule of glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate. Only G3P continues through the remainder of glycolysis, so this step effectively doubles the yield of downstream products from each glucose.

### 3.2 Energy Payoff Phase (Steps 6–10)

**Step 6: Oxidation and phosphorylation of G3P**  
*Enzyme: glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate dehydrogenase*

$$
\text{Glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate} + \text{NAD}^+ + \text{P}_i \rightleftharpoons 1,3\text{-bisphosphoglycerate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+
$$

The aldehyde group of G3P is oxidised to a carboxyl group, with the energy of oxidation driving the formation of a high‑energy acyl phosphate. NAD$^+$ is reduced to NADH. This is the only step of glycolysis in which NAD$^+$ is reduced.

**Step 7: First substrate‑level phosphorylation**  
*Enzyme: phosphoglycerate kinase*

$$
1,3\text{-bisphosphoglycerate} + \text{ADP} \rightleftharpoons 3\text{-phosphoglycerate} + \text{ATP}
$$

The high‑energy phosphate is transferred directly to ADP, forming ATP. Because two G3P molecules enter this step per glucose, two ATP are produced here. The reaction is reversible but pulled forward by the rapid removal of ATP.

**Step 8: Phosphate shift**  
*Enzyme: phosphoglycerate mutase*

$$
3\text{-phosphoglycerate} \rightleftharpoons 2\text{-phosphoglycerate}
$$

The phosphoryl group is relocated from C‑3 to C‑2, priming the molecule for dehydration.

**Step 9: Dehydration**  
*Enzyme: enolase*

$$
2\text{-phosphoglycerate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

Enolase removes a water molecule, generating a high‑energy enol phosphate. PEP has a very high phosphoryl‑transfer potential, making the following step thermodynamically favourable.

**Step 10: Second substrate‑level phosphorylation**  
*Enzyme: pyruvate kinase*

$$
\text{Phosphoenolpyruvate} + \text{ADP} + \text{H}^+ \xrightarrow{\text{Mg}^{2+},\,\text{K}^+} \text{Pyruvate} + \text{ATP}
$$

The transfer of the phosphate from PEP to ADP is irreversible and yields pyruvate (in its enol form, which quickly tautomerises to the keto form). Again, two ATP are generated per glucose. Pyruvate kinase is allosterically activated by fructose‑1,6‑bisphosphate (feed‑forward activation) and inhibited by ATP and acetyl‑CoA. It is also regulated by covalent phosphorylation in response to glucagon signalling in the liver.

## 4. Energetics and Regulation

Under standard conditions, the conversion of glucose to two pyruvate molecules is exergonic:

$$
\Delta G^{\circ\prime} \approx -73.3\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}
$$

The invested ATP (2) are more than compensated by the ATP produced (4), yielding a net of 2 ATP. Additionally, 2 NADH are generated and can be reoxidised to yield further ATP in aerobic organisms (via the electron transport chain).

Regulation of glycolysis occurs primarily at three irreversible steps:

- **Hexokinase/glucokinase**: controls glucose entry into the pathway.
- **Phosphofructokinase‑1 (PFK‑1)**: the most important control point; responds to cellular energy charge.
- **Pyruvate kinase**: adjusts the output of the pathway.

In addition, hormonal signals (insulin and glucagon) regulate the synthesis and activity of key glycolytic enzymes, especially in liver, integrating glycolysis with whole‑body energy homeostasis.

## 5. Examples and Connections

### 5.1 Yeast Fermentation
In the absence of oxygen, yeast cells convert pyruvate to ethanol and CO$_2$ in order to regenerate NAD$^+$ from NADH, allowing glycolysis to continue. The net reaction:

$$
\text{Glucose} + 2\,\text{ADP} + 2\,\text{P}_i \rightarrow 2\,\text{Ethanol} + 2\,\text{CO}_2 + 2\,\text{ATP} + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

This process is the basis of bread making and alcoholic beverage production.

### 5.2 Muscle Cells Under Anaerobic Conditions
When oxygen is scarce (e.g., during intense exercise), animal muscle cells reduce pyruvate to lactate, catalysed by lactate dehydrogenase:

$$
\text{Pyruvate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{Lactate} + \text{NAD}^+
$$

This regenerates the NAD$^+$ needed for step 6, enabling continued glycolytic ATP production. The accumulated lactate is later converted back to glucose in the liver (Cori cycle) or oxidised when oxygen becomes available.

### 5.3 Glycolysis in Cancer Cells
Many tumour cells exhibit the Warburg effect: they rely on high levels of glycolysis followed by lactate fermentation even in the presence of oxygen (aerobic glycolysis). This metabolic shift supports rapid biosynthesis of macromolecules and is being exploited for diagnostic imaging (PET scans using $^{18}$F‑fluorodeoxyglucose).

## 6. Summary

Glycolysis is a universal, cytosolic pathway that breaks down one glucose molecule into two pyruvate molecules. It consists of ten enzyme‑catalysed steps, organised into an energy‑investment phase (consuming 2 ATP) and an energy‑payoff phase (producing 4 ATP and 2 NADH). The net energy yield is 2 ATP and 2 NADH per glucose.

The pathway is tightly regulated at three irreversible steps, primarily by the allosteric enzyme phosphofructokinase‑1, to align glucose catabolism with the cell’s energy status. Beyond energy production, glycolysis provides carbon skeletons for biosynthetic pathways and intermediates that link to the pentose phosphate pathway and the tricarboxylic acid cycle.

Depending on the availability of oxygen and the organism’s metabolic repertoire, the fate of pyruvate varies: it can enter the mitochondria for complete oxidation, be fermented to ethanol or lactate, or be used in anabolic reactions. Understanding glycolysis is fundamental to grasping cellular respiration, fermentation, and the metabolic adaptations observed in health and disease.
