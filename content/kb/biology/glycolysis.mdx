# Glycolysis

## Introduction
Glycolysis (from the Greek *glykys*, “sweet,” and *lysis*, “splitting”) is the universal, evolutionarily ancient metabolic pathway that converts one molecule of glucose into two molecules of pyruvate. Occurring in the cytosol of nearly every living cell, glycolysis does not require oxygen and proceeds under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions. It serves three major purposes:

- **ATP production** by substrate‑level phosphorylation (net 2 ATP per glucose).
- **Reducing power** in the form of NADH (2 per glucose), which can be fed into oxidative phosphorylation or used to regenerate NAD⁺ in fermentation.
- **Carbon skeletons** for biosynthesis (pyruvate can be further metabolised to acetyl‑CoA, lactate, ethanol, and many other intermediates).

The overall balanced equation for glycolysis is:

$$
\text{Glucose} + 2\,\text{NAD}^{+} + 2\,\text{ADP} + 2\,\text{P}_i \rightarrow 2\,\text{Pyruvate} + 2\,\text{NADH} + 2\,\text{H}^{+} + 2\,\text{ATP} + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

In eukaryotes, the pyruvate produced is transported into mitochondria for complete oxidation to CO₂ and H₂O when O₂ is present, or fermented to lactate or ethanol when O₂ is limiting. Because glycolysis can support ATP synthesis independently of mitochondria, it is especially critical for rapidly contracting skeletal muscle, red blood cells (which lack mitochondria), and many tumours that exhibit the Warburg effect.

## The 10 Steps of Glycolysis

Glycolysis is traditionally divided into two phases: the **energy investment (preparatory) phase**, which consumes 2 ATP to phosphorylate glucose and split it into two triose phosphates, and the **energy payoff phase**, which oxidises the trioses and generates 4 ATP and 2 NADH. The ten enzyme‑catalysed reactions are:

### Energy Investment Phase

1. **Hexokinase** (or glucokinase in liver) phosphorylates glucose, trapping it inside the cell:  
   $$
   \text{Glucose} + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{Glucose‑6‑phosphate} + \text{ADP}
   $$

2. **Phosphoglucose isomerase** converts the aldose to the ketose:  
   $$
   \text{Glucose‑6‑phosphate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Fructose‑6‑phosphate}
   $$

3. **Phosphofructokinase‑1 (PFK‑1)** catalyses the first committed (irreversible) step unique to glycolysis:  
   $$
   \text{Fructose‑6‑phosphate} + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{Fructose‑1,6‑bisphosphate} + \text{ADP}
   $$

4. **Aldolase** cleaves the six‑carbon bisphosphate into two three‑carbon fragments:  
   $$
   \text{Fructose‑1,6‑bisphosphate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP)} + \text{Glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate (G3P)}
   $$

5. **Triose phosphate isomerase** rapidly interconverts DHAP and G3P, allowing both halves of the glucose molecule to proceed through the remaining reactions. At equilibrium, ~96% is in the DHAP form, but G3P is continually removed by step 6, pulling the overall conversion to 2 G3P per glucose:  
   $$
   \text{DHAP} \rightleftharpoons \text{G3P}
   $$

### Energy Payoff Phase
From this point, every reaction occurs **twice** per original glucose because two molecules of G3P are processed.

6. **Glyceraldehyde‑3‑phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH)** oxidises G3P and incorporates inorganic phosphate, producing the first high‑energy intermediate and NADH:  
   $$
   \text{G3P} + \text{NAD}^{+} + \text{P}_i \rightleftharpoons \text{1,3‑Bisphosphoglycerate (1,3‑BPG)} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^{+}
   $$

7. **Phosphoglycerate kinase** performs substrate‑level phosphorylation, transferring the high‑energy phosphoryl group of 1,3‑BPG to ADP:  
   $$
   \text{1,3‑BPG} + \text{ADP} \rightleftharpoons \text{3‑Phosphoglycerate} + \text{ATP}
   $$

8. **Phosphoglycerate mutase** relocates the phosphate from C‑3 to C‑2:  
   $$
   \text{3‑Phosphoglycerate} \rightleftharpoons \text{2‑Phosphoglycerate}
   $$

9. **Enolase** dehydrates 2‑phosphoglycerate, creating another high‑energy compound, phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP):  
   $$
   \text{2‑Phosphoglycerate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}
   $$

10. **Pyruvate kinase** catalyses the second substrate‑level phosphorylation, yielding pyruvate and ATP. This step is irreversible under cellular conditions:  
    $$
    \text{PEP} + \text{ADP} \rightarrow \text{Pyruvate} + \text{ATP}
    $$

#### Net Yield per Glucose
| Molecule | Investment | Payoff | Net |
|----------|------------|--------|-----|
| ATP      | –2         | +4     | +2  |
| NADH     | 0          | +2     | +2  |
| Pyruvate | 0          | +2     | +2  |
| H₂O      | 0          | +2 (step 9) | +2 |

Note that the NADH produced must be re‑oxidised to NAD⁺ for glycolysis to continue. Under aerobic conditions, mitochondrial shuttles (e.g., malate‑aspartate shuttle) deliver the electrons to the electron‑transport chain, yielding up to ~5 ATP per glucose (via glycerol‑3‑phosphate shuttle) or ~7 ATP (via malate‑aspartate shuttle) depending on tissue. Anaerobically, NADH is recycled by fermentation.

## Regulation of Glycolysis

Glycolysis is tightly controlled at three irreversible steps to match cellular energy needs and biosynthetic demands.

- **Hexokinase/Glucokinase:** Hexokinase is inhibited by its product, glucose‑6‑phosphate. Glucokinase (liver, pancreatic β‑cells) has a lower affinity for glucose and is induced by insulin, enabling the liver to take up glucose only when blood levels are high.

- **Phosphofructokinase‑1 (PFK‑1):** The major rate‑limiting enzyme.  
  - **Inhibited** by high ATP, citrate (a TCA cycle intermediate) – signals abundant energy.  
  - **Activated** by AMP (low energy charge) and fructose‑2,6‑bisphosphate, a potent allosteric activator produced by PFK‑2 in response to insulin. Glucagon signalling reduces fructose‑2,6‑bisphosphate levels in liver, slowing glycolysis.

- **Pyruvate kinase:**  
  - **Inhibited** by ATP, alanine (biosynthetic feedback), and covalent phosphorylation by glucagon (in liver).  
  - **Activated** by fructose‑1,6‑bisphosphate (feed‑forward activation from earlier in the pathway).

These regulatory mechanisms ensure glycolysis is active when energy is needed and turned down when ATP is plentiful or alternative fuels are available.

## Fates of Pyruvate

The endpoint of glycolysis, pyruvate, can take several metabolic routes depending on the cell type and oxygen availability.

- **Aerobic oxidation (most tissues, mitochondrial):**  
  Pyruvate is transported into mitochondria and oxidized by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex to acetyl‑CoA, which then enters the citric acid cycle. The NADH from glycolysis and the subsequent oxidation of acetyl‑CoA yield up to ~30–32 ATP per glucose in total.

- **Lactic acid fermentation (anaerobic muscle, red blood cells, some microorganisms):**  
  Lactate dehydrogenase reduces pyruvate to lactate using NADH:  
  $$
  \text{Pyruvate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^{+} \rightarrow \text{Lactate} + \text{NAD}^{+}
  $$
  This regenerates NAD⁺, allowing continuous ATP production by glycolysis during intense exercise. Accumulated lactate is later reconverted to glucose in the liver (Cori cycle).

- **Alcoholic fermentation (yeast and some plants under hypoxia):**  
  Pyruvate is first decarboxylated to acetaldehyde, then reduced to ethanol:
  $$
  \text{Pyruvate} \rightarrow \text{Acetaldehyde} + \text{CO}_2 \\
  \text{Acetaldehyde} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^{+} \rightarrow \text{Ethanol} + \text{NAD}^{+}
  $$
  This process is exploited in baking and brewing.

### Example: Human Sprinting
During a 100‑m sprint, skeletal muscle rapidly hydrolyses ATP, raising ADP and AMP. PFK‑1 is activated, glycolysis runs at maximal rate, and the large NADH production exceeds the mitochondrial capacity. Lactate fermentation provides the NAD⁺ needed to sustain ATP generation. The resulting lactate accumulation contributes to the burning sensation and temporary muscle acidosis, but provides the energy burst.

## Clinical and Evolutionary Significance

- **Ancient pathway:** Glycolysis is present in all three domains of life, highlighting its origin before the rise of free atmospheric oxygen. Many of its enzymes are highly conserved across species.

- **Metabolic diseases:** Deficiencies in glycolytic enzymes, particularly pyruvate kinase, cause nonspherocytic haemolytic anaemia because red blood cells rely solely on glycolysis for ATP to maintain ion gradients and membrane integrity.

- **Cancer metabolism (Warburg effect):** Many cancer cells upregulate glycolysis even in the presence of oxygen (aerobic glycolysis). This shift is driven by oncogenic signalling (e.g., HIF‑1α, MYC) and allows rapid ATP production and synthesis of nucleotide, amino acid, and lipid precursors, supporting proliferation. The phenomenon is exploited in PET imaging with ¹⁸F‑fluorodeoxyglucose.

## Summary

Glycolysis is a ten‑enzyme cytosolic pathway that breaks one glucose (6 C) into two pyruvate (3 C) molecules, yielding a net profit of 2 ATP and 2 NADH. It consists of an energy investment phase (consuming 2 ATP) and an energy payoff phase (generating 4 ATP). The pathway is regulated primarily at hexokinase, PFK‑1, and pyruvate kinase to match cellular energy charge. Pyruvate produced can be aerobically oxidised in the mitochondria or fermented to lactate or ethanol to regenerate NAD⁺, enabling glycolysis to continue during oxygen limitation. Understanding glycolysis is fundamental to biochemistry, medicine, and the study of cellular energy metabolism.
