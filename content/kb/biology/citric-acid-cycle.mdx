# The Citric Acid Cycle

## Introduction
The citric acid cycle—also known as the Krebs cycle or tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle—is a central metabolic hub of aerobic respiration. Occurring in the mitochondrial matrix of eukaryotic cells, it completes the oxidation of acetyl-CoA derived from carbohydrates, fatty acids, and amino acids. The cycle was elucidated by Hans Krebs in 1937 and represents a closed loop of eight enzyme‑catalyzed reactions that harvest high‑energy electrons, producing $\text{NADH}$, $\text{FADH}_2$, and one $\text{GTP}$ (or $\text{ATP}$) per turn. These reduced coenzymes subsequently feed the electron transport chain, driving oxidative phosphorylation.

Beyond energy production, the cycle provides biosynthetic precursors for amino acids, heme, and nucleotides, making it an amphibolic pathway. Its regulation is tightly coupled to the cell’s energy status, ensuring a precise balance between catabolism and anabolism.

## Core Concepts

### Overview of the Cycle
Each turn of the cycle begins with the two‑carbon acetyl group of acetyl‑CoA ($\text{CH}_3\text{CO-SCoA}$) condensing with the four‑carbon oxaloacetate to form the six‑carbon citrate. Through a series of decarboxylations, dehydrogenations, and a substrate‑level phosphorylation, two carbons are lost as $\text{CO}_2$, and the original oxaloacetate is regenerated. The net reaction per acetyl‑CoA is:

$$\text{Acetyl-CoA} + 3\,\text{NAD}^+ + \text{FAD} + \text{GDP} + \text{P}_i + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\,\text{CO}_2 + 3\,\text{NADH} + \text{FADH}_2 + \text{GTP} + 2\,\text{H}^+ + \text{CoA-SH}$$

All reactions take place in the mitochondrial matrix except succinate dehydrogenase, which is embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane (Complex II of the electron transport chain).

### Step‑by‑Step Reactions

#### 1. Citrate Synthase (Condensation)
Acetyl‑CoA (2C) combines with oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C). This irreversible reaction is a Claisen condensation.

$$\text{Acetyl-CoA} + \text{Oxaloacetate} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{citrate synthase}} \text{Citrate} + \text{CoA-SH}$$

Citrate synthase is regulated by $\text{ATP}$, $\text{NADH}$, and citrate (inhibitors) and is activated by $\text{ADP}$.

#### 2. Aconitase (Isomerization)
Citrate is isomerized to isocitrate via the intermediate *cis*‑aconitate. Aconitase contains an iron‑sulfur cluster that facilitates the dehydration and rehydration steps.

$$\text{Citrate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Isocitrate}$$

#### 3. Isocitrate Dehydrogenase (Oxidative Decarboxylation)
Isocitrate is oxidatively decarboxylated to α‑ketoglutarate (5C). This is the first $\text{NADH}$‑producing step and the first $\text{CO}_2$ release. The reaction is essentially irreversible and a major control point.

$$\text{Isocitrate} + \text{NAD}^+ \xrightarrow{\text{isocitrate dehydrogenase}} \alpha\text{-Ketoglutarate} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+$$

The enzyme is allosterically activated by $\text{ADP}$ and $\text{Ca}^{2+}$, and inhibited by $\text{ATP}$ and $\text{NADH}$.

#### 4. α‑Ketoglutarate Dehydrogenase Complex (Oxidative Decarboxylation)
This multi‑enzyme complex (analogous to pyruvate dehydrogenase) converts α‑ketoglutarate (5C) to succinyl‑CoA (4C), releasing the second $\text{CO}_2$ and generating the second $\text{NADH}$. Cofactors include thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP), lipoic acid, $\text{CoA}$, $\text{FAD}$, and $\text{NAD}^+$.

$$\alpha\text{-Ketoglutarate} + \text{NAD}^+ + \text{CoA-SH} \rightarrow \text{Succinyl-CoA} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+$$

This step is also highly regulated, inhibited by $\text{NADH}$, $\text{ATP}$, and succinyl‑CoA, and activated by $\text{Ca}^{2+}$.

#### 5. Succinyl‑CoA Synthetase (Substrate‑Level Phosphorylation)
The thioester bond of succinyl‑CoA is cleaved, and the energy is coupled to the synthesis of $\text{GTP}$ (or $\text{ATP}$ in some organisms). This is the only direct high‑energy phosphate produced in the cycle.

$$\text{Succinyl-CoA} + \text{GDP} + \text{P}_i \rightleftharpoons \text{Succinate} + \text{GTP} + \text{CoA-SH}$$

In mammalian cells, the enzyme is specific for $\text{GDP}$, forming $\text{GTP}$, which can be converted to $\text{ATP}$ by nucleoside diphosphate kinase.

#### 6. Succinate Dehydrogenase (Flavin‑Dependent Oxidation)
Succinate is oxidized to fumarate, with $\text{FAD}$ as the electron acceptor. The enzyme is part of Complex II of the electron transport chain, directly linking the cycle to the inner mitochondrial membrane.

$$\text{Succinate} + \text{FAD} \rightarrow \text{Fumarate} + \text{FADH}_2$$

#### 7. Fumarase (Hydration)
Fumarate is hydrated to L‑malate. The enzyme is stereospecific, producing only the L‑isomer.

$$\text{Fumarate} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{L-Malate}$$

#### 8. Malate Dehydrogenase (Regeneration of Oxaloacetate)
L‑Malate is oxidized to oxaloacetate, generating the third $\text{NADH}$. Although the equilibrium lies strongly toward malate, the reaction is pulled forward by the continuous consumption of oxaloacetate in the first step.

$$\text{L-Malate} + \text{NAD}^+ \xrightarrow{\text{malate dehydrogenase}} \text{Oxaloacetate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+$$

### Energy Yield and ATP Equivalents
Each turn of the cycle directly yields:
- $3\,\text{NADH}$
- $1\,\text{FADH}_2$
- $1\,\text{GTP}$ (≈ $1\,\text{ATP}$)

The $\text{NADH}$ and $\text{FADH}_2$ are oxidized by the electron transport chain, producing approximately $2.5$ and $1.5$ molecules of $\text{ATP}$, respectively. Thus, per acetyl‑CoA, the total ATP yield is about $10$ ATP equivalents ($7.5$ from $\text{NADH}$, $1.5$ from $\text{FADH}_2$, $1$ from $\text{GTP}$). For one glucose molecule (yielding two acetyl‑CoA), the citric acid cycle contributes roughly $20$ ATP equivalents, in addition to those from glycolysis and pyruvate oxidation.

### Regulation of the Cycle
The citric acid cycle is controlled primarily at three irreversible steps:
- **Citrate synthase**: inhibited by $\text{ATP}$, $\text{NADH}$, citrate; activated by $\text{ADP}$.
- **Isocitrate dehydrogenase**: allosterically stimulated by $\text{ADP}$ and $\text{Ca}^{2+}$; inhibited by $\text{ATP}$ and $\text{NADH}$.
- **α‑Ketoglutarate dehydrogenase**: inhibited by $\text{NADH}$, $\text{ATP}$, and succinyl‑CoA; activated by $\text{Ca}^{2+}$.

These feedback mechanisms ensure that the cycle operates at a rate matching the cell’s demand for $\text{ATP}$. High energy charge signals (high $\text{ATP}/\text{ADP}$, high $\text{NADH}/\text{NAD}^+$) slow the cycle, while low energy charge and increased $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ (e.g., during muscle contraction) accelerate it.

### Anaplerotic Reactions
Since intermediates of the cycle are withdrawn for biosynthesis (e.g., oxaloacetate for gluconeogenesis, α‑ketoglutarate for amino acids), they must be replenished. Anaplerotic (“filling up”) reactions restore cycle components. The most important is the pyruvate carboxylase reaction:

$$\text{Pyruvate} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{ATP} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{pyruvate carboxylase}} \text{Oxaloacetate} + \text{ADP} + \text{P}_i$$

This reaction is activated by acetyl‑CoA, ensuring that oxaloacetate is available when acetyl‑CoA is abundant. Other anaplerotic pathways include the conversion of glutamate to α‑ketoglutarate and the degradation of odd‑chain fatty acids to succinyl‑CoA.

## Examples

### Integration of Fuel Metabolism
After a carbohydrate‑rich meal, glucose is oxidized via glycolysis to pyruvate, which enters mitochondria as acetyl‑CoA. High insulin and low glucagon favor dephosphorylation (activation) of pyruvate dehydrogenase, feeding acetyl‑CoA into the cycle. The resulting citrate and $\text{ATP}$ inhibit glycolysis, and surplus citrate is exported to the cytosol for fatty acid synthesis.

During fasting, fatty acids become the primary fuel. β‑Oxidation generates large amounts of acetyl‑CoA, which stimulates pyruvate carboxylase, ensuring sufficient oxaloacetate to sustain the cycle. In prolonged starvation, the liver may convert acetyl‑CoA to ketone bodies when oxaloacetate is depleted for gluconeogenesis, demonstrating the cycle’s pivotal role in metabolic flexibility.

### Clinical Connection: Beriberi and Thiamine Deficiency
Thiamine (vitamin B1) is a precursor for TPP, an essential cofactor of the α‑ketoglutarate dehydrogenase complex. Thiamine deficiency impairs the citric acid cycle, reducing $\text{NADH}$ and $\text{ATP}$ production. This manifests as neurological and cardiovascular symptoms characteristic of beriberi. The accumulation of α‑ketoglutarate and other metabolites can be used diagnostically.

## Summary
The citric acid cycle is a fundamental catabolic pathway that:
- Completely oxidizes acetyl‑CoA to $\text{CO}_2$.
- Captures energy as $3\,\text{NADH}$, $1\,\text{FADH}_2$, and $1\,\text{GTP}$ per turn.
- Provides metabolic intermediates for biosynthesis (amphibolic role).
- Is rigorously controlled by the cell’s energy charge ($\text{ATP}$/$\text{ADP}$, $\text{NADH}$/$\text{NAD}^+$) and calcium signals.
- Relies on anaplerotic reactions to maintain cycle integrity.

Understanding the citric acid cycle is essential for grasping how cells extract energy from diverse nutrients and how metabolic dysregulation underlies diseases such as diabetes, cancer, and mitochondrial disorders.
