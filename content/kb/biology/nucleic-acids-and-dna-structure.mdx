# Nucleic Acids and DNA Structure

## Introduction

Nucleic acids are biopolymers essential for the storage, transmission, and expression of genetic information in all living organisms. The two primary forms are deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA). DNA serves as the long-term repository of genetic instructions, while RNA plays diverse roles in gene expression and regulation. Understanding the structure of nucleic acids, particularly the iconic double-helical architecture of DNA, is fundamental to molecular biology. This article provides a comprehensive overview of nucleic acid chemistry, from the monomeric nucleotides to the three-dimensional conformation of the DNA duplex, highlighting key principles, quantitative dimensions, and biological significance.

## Core Concepts

### Nucleotide Structure

Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides. Each nucleotide consists of three components:

1. **A nitrogenous base** – a heterocyclic aromatic ring containing nitrogen.
2. **A pentose sugar** – a five-carbon monosaccharide.
3. **One or more phosphate groups** – linked to the sugar via ester bonds.

The nitrogenous bases are divided into two families:

- **Purines**: adenine (A) and guanine (G), characterized by a double-ring structure (a six-membered ring fused to a five-membered ring).
- **Pyrimidines**: cytosine (C), thymine (T) in DNA, and uracil (U) in RNA, possessing a single six-membered ring.

The sugar in DNA is 2′-deoxyribose, which lacks a hydroxyl group at the 2′ carbon; RNA contains ribose, with a hydroxyl at the 2′ position. The base is attached to the 1′ carbon of the sugar via an $N$-glycosidic bond (N9 of purines, N1 of pyrimidines), forming a **nucleoside**. Addition of one or more phosphate groups to the 5′ carbon yields a **nucleotide** (e.g., adenosine monophosphate, AMP). Nucleotides can exist as mono-, di-, or triphosphates, with the triphosphate forms (ATP, GTP, etc.) serving as energy currency and substrates for nucleic acid synthesis.

### Polynucleotide Formation

Nucleotides polymerize through phosphodiester bonds, where the 5′-phosphate group of one nucleotide is linked to the 3′-hydroxyl group of the sugar of the preceding nucleotide. This creates a backbone with alternating sugar and phosphate residues, from which the bases project. The resulting strand has a directional polarity: a free 5′ end (usually phosphorylated) and a free 3′ end (hydroxyl). By convention, sequences are written from the 5′ end to the 3′ end (e.g., 5′-ATGC-3′).

Mathematically, a polynucleotide chain of length $n$ nucleotides contains $n-1$ phosphodiester linkages. The repeat unit along the backbone consists of six backbone bonds (P–O5′, O5′–C5′, C5′–C4′, C4′–C3′, C3′–O3′, O3′–P), with the glycosidic bond (C1′–N) orienting the base.

### DNA Double Helix

The Watson–Crick model of DNA, proposed in 1953, is a right-handed double helix composed of two antiparallel polynucleotide strands wrapped around a common axis. The hydrophilic sugar–phosphate backbones lie on the exterior, while the hydrophobic bases are stacked in the interior, perpendicular to the helix axis. The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs and by base-stacking interactions.

#### Base Pairing and Chargaff’s Rules

The specificity of base pairing arises from the geometry and hydrogen-bonding capacity of the bases. Adenine pairs with thymine (DNA) or uracil (RNA) through two hydrogen bonds; guanine pairs with cytosine through three hydrogen bonds. This complementarity explains Chargaff’s rules, which state that for any double-stranded DNA:

$$
[A] = [T], \quad [G] = [C]
$$

and consequently:

$$
[A] + [G] = [T] + [C] \quad \text{(purine = pyrimidine)}
$$

These equalities are fundamental to DNA replication and transcription.

#### Structural Dimensions

Key quantitative parameters of B-DNA (the physiological form) are:

- Helix pitch (rise per complete turn): $3.4\ \text{nm}$
- Number of base pairs per turn: $10$ (10.5 in solution)
- Rise per base pair: $0.34\ \text{nm}$
- Rotation per base pair: $36^\circ$ ($\frac{360^\circ}{10}$)
- Diameter of the helix: $2.0\ \text{nm}$
- Distance between the backbones: approximately $2.0\ \text{nm}$, with major groove width $\sim 2.2\ \text{nm}$ and minor groove width $\sim 1.2\ \text{nm}$

These dimensions can be expressed mathematically. The axial rise $h$ per base pair step is constant, so the length $L$ of a DNA segment with $N$ base pairs is $L = N \times 0.34\ \text{nm}$. For example, the human haploid genome of $\sim 3 \times 10^9$ base pairs would stretch to about $L = 3 \times 10^9 \times 0.34\ \text{nm} \approx 1.02\ \text{m}$ if fully extended.

#### Major and Minor Grooves

The asymmetry of the glycosidic bonds and the arrangement of the base pairs create two grooves of unequal width that spiral along the helix. The **major groove** is wider and exposes chemical groups (hydrogen bond donors/acceptors, methyl groups) that allow sequence-specific recognition by proteins. The **minor groove** is narrower and contains subtler patterns of hydration and electrostatic potential. The dimensions are a direct consequence of the helix geometry; the angle between the glycosidic bonds of a base pair is $\sim 120^\circ$ for the major groove side and $\sim 240^\circ$ for the minor groove side.

#### Base Stacking and Stability

While hydrogen bonds provide specificity, the overall stability of the DNA duplex is largely driven by hydrophobic and van der Waals stacking interactions between adjacent base rings. The $\pi$-electrons of the aromatic bases create induced dipoles that contribute approximately $-4\ \text{to}\ -15\ \text{kJ/mol}$ per base-pair step, depending on sequence. Stacking also enforces the planar, nearly perpendicular orientation of the bases to the helix axis, with a slight propeller twist.

### DNA Conformations

DNA can adopt multiple secondary structures depending on hydration, ionic conditions, and sequence. The three most common are:

- **B-DNA**: The standard right-handed double helix described above, with 10.5 bp/turn in solution, a wide major groove, and a narrow minor groove. This is the predominant form under physiological conditions.
- **A-DNA**: A shorter, wider right-handed helix with 11 bp/turn, bases tilted relative to the axis, and a deep, narrow major groove. Observed in dehydrated samples and in RNA duplexes and DNA–RNA hybrids.
- **Z-DNA**: A left-handed helix with a zigzag backbone conformation, 12 bp/turn, and a very narrow major groove. Favored by alternating purine–pyrimidine sequences (e.g., d(CG)$_n$) under high salt or methylation. Its biological role is linked to transcription regulation and genome instability.

These conformational variations illustrate the structural plasticity of nucleic acids, with biological implications for protein recognition and gene expression.

### Example: Calculating Physical Properties of a DNA Sequence

Consider a 153-base-pair double-stranded DNA fragment.  
- Length: $153 \times 0.34\ \text{nm} = 52.02\ \text{nm}$.  
- Number of helical turns: $153 / 10.5 \approx 14.57$ turns (assuming solution form).  
- If the sequence has 60 adenine residues in one strand, then $[A]=60$, $[T]=60$ by complementarity. If $[G]=40$ and $[C]=40$, Chargaff’s rule holds: total purines $60+40=100$, total pyrimidines $60+40=100$, and $A+T$ vs $G+C$ composition can be calculated. The $T_m$ (melting temperature) can be estimated from the GC content, as G–C pairs contribute more to stability (three H-bonds) than A–T pairs (two H-bonds).

Such examples illustrate the direct application of structural principles to experimental data.

## Summary

Nucleic acids, composed of nucleotide monomers, form the molecular basis of heredity. DNA’s double-helical structure, with its antiparallel sugar–phosphate backbones and complementary base pairing (A–T, G–C), elegantly explains the mechanisms of replication and information storage. The physical dimensions of B-DNA—0.34 nm rise per base pair, 3.4 nm pitch, 2.0 nm diameter—provide precise constraints for cellular processes. Chargaff’s rule ($[A]=[T]$, $[G]=[C]$) is a direct consequence of base complementarity. Structural features such as major and minor grooves enable sequence-specific protein–DNA interactions, while alternative conformations (A-DNA, Z-DNA) reveal functional versatility. A firm grasp of nucleic acid chemistry and DNA topology is essential for understanding genome organization, gene regulation, and modern biotechnologies.
