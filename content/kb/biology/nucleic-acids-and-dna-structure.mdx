# Nucleic Acids and DNA Structure

## Introduction
Nucleic acids are the biomolecules responsible for the storage, transmission, and expression of genetic information in all living organisms. They are linear polymers of nucleotides, which encode the instructions needed for the synthesis of proteins and functional RNAs. The two naturally occurring types are deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA). DNA serves as the long‑term repository of genetic information, while RNA plays diverse roles including messenger, transfer, and catalytic functions. Understanding the structure of nucleic acids, particularly the double‑helical architecture of DNA, is fundamental to modern biology, because structure directly dictates how genetic information is copied, read, and regulated.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Nucleotides: The Monomeric Units
Each nucleotide consists of three components:
- A **nitrogenous base**: a heterocyclic aromatic ring structure containing nitrogen.
- A **pentose sugar**: a five‑carbon monosaccharide (ribose in RNA, 2′‑deoxyribose in DNA).
- One or more **phosphate groups** attached to the sugar.

The nitrogenous bases are divided into two families:
- **Purines**: adenine (A) and guanine (G) – a fused double‑ring system.
- **Pyrimidines**: cytosine (C), thymine (T) – found in DNA – and uracil (U) – found in RNA. Thymine is 5‑methyluracil.

| Base       | Symbol | Type       | Found in |
|------------|--------|------------|----------|
| Adenine    | A      | Purine     | DNA, RNA |
| Guanine    | G      | Purine     | DNA, RNA |
| Cytosine   | C      | Pyrimidine | DNA, RNA |
| Thymine    | T      | Pyrimidine | DNA      |
| Uracil     | U      | Pyrimidine | RNA      |

The sugar in DNA lacks a hydroxyl (–OH) group at the 2′ position, hence “deoxy”. The carbon atoms of the sugar are numbered with primes (1′ to 5′) to distinguish them from atoms in the base.

Nucleotides are linked by **phosphodiester bonds** between the 3′ hydroxyl of one sugar and the 5′ phosphate of the next, forming a strand with a distinct directionality. By convention, a nucleic acid sequence is written from the 5′ end to the 3′ end, e.g., 5′‑ATGCCGT‑3′.

### 2. Chargaff’s Rules and Base Composition
Erwin Chargaff observed that in double‑stranded DNA the amount of adenine equals thymine, and the amount of guanine equals cytosine. This equivalence, known as Chargaff’s rules, can be expressed as:

$$A = T \quad \text{and} \quad G = C$$

Furthermore, the total purine content equals the total pyrimidine content:

$$A + G = T + C$$

**Example calculation**  
If a sample of double‑stranded DNA contains 30% adenine, what are the percentages of the other bases?

Using Chargaff’s rules:
- $A = T = 30\%$
- $A + T = 60\%$
- Therefore $G + C = 100\% - 60\% = 40\%$
- Since $G = C$, each must be $20\%$.

$$T = 30\%, \quad G = 20\%, \quad C = 20\%$$

These equalities arise directly from the base‑pairing pattern in the double helix.

### 3. Primary Structure: The Nucleotide Sequence
The primary structure of a nucleic acid is defined by the linear order of its nucleotides, joined by 3′→5′ phosphodiester linkages. The phosphate‑sugar backbones extend along the outside of the chain, while the bases project inward. The sequence carries the genetic information; a single nucleotide change can alter a gene’s function.

Formally, each phosphodiester bond forms via a condensation reaction that releases a water molecule. The repeating unit along the backbone is a **phosphate‑sugar‑phosphate** chain. The negative charges on the phosphates give DNA its polyanionic character and influence its interactions with proteins and cations.

### 4. Secondary Structure: The Watson‑Crick Double Helix
The most stable form of DNA is the right‑handed double helix (B‑DNA) proposed by Watson and Crick in 1953, based on X‑ray diffraction data from Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins. Key features include:

- **Two antiparallel strands**: one runs 5′→3′, the complementary strand runs 3′→5′.
- **Base pairing specificity**: A pairs with T via two hydrogen bonds; G pairs with C via three hydrogen bonds. This complementarity ensures faithful replication.
- **Hydrogen bonds and base stacking**: The stacked, planar aromatic rings of adjacent bases contribute van der Waals and hydrophobic interactions, stabilising the helix.
- **Dimensions**: The helix diameter is about 2 nm. Each full turn spans 10 base pairs (bp), a rise of 3.4 nm. Therefore the axial rise per base pair is 0.34 nm.
- **Major and minor grooves**: The asymmetric spacing of the sugar‑phosphate backbones creates two grooves of unequal width. The major groove exposes more chemical information from the bases, allowing sequence‑specific binding by proteins (e.g., transcription factors).

The two strands are complementary but not identical. The sequence of one strand determines the sequence of the other. For a given segment 5′‑ATCGGCA‑3′, its complement is 3′‑TAGCCGT‑5′ (written 5′‑ACGGCTA‑3′ if reversed). This complementarity is the basis for replication, transcription, and many molecular biology techniques.

**Mathematical complementarity**  
If one strand has a base composition $A_1$, $T_1$, $G_1$, $C_1$, the complementary strand must have:

$$A_2 = T_1, \quad T_2 = A_1, \quad G_2 = C_1, \quad C_2 = G_1$$

### 5. Alternative DNA Conformations
Under different ionic conditions or sequence contexts, DNA can adopt alternative right‑handed (A‑DNA) or left‑handed (Z‑DNA) conformations. A‑DNA is a shorter, wider helix often observed in dehydrated samples or RNA‑DNA hybrids. Z‑DNA is a left‑handed helix with a zigzag backbone, favoured by alternating purine‑pyrimidine tracts (e.g., 5′‑GCGCGC‑3′). While B‑DNA is the predominant physiological form, local Z‑DNA regions may play roles in gene regulation.

### 6. Tertiary Structure: Supercoiling and Chromosome Organisation
In cells, the linear double helix is further compacted by twisting and folding. **Supercoiling** results from overwinding or underwinding the helix, creating torsional strain. Enzymes called topoisomerases regulate supercoiling, which is essential for DNA replication and transcription.

- In prokaryotes, the circular chromosome is organised into a nucleoid with supercoiled loops.
- In eukaryotes, DNA wraps around histone proteins to form nucleosomes, the fundamental units of chromatin. Strings of nucleosomes fold into chromatin fibres, which are further compacted into chromosomes during cell division. This hierarchical packaging reduces the effective length of DNA by a factor of ~10,000 and plays a critical role in gene regulation through epigenetic modifications.

### 7. RNA: Structural Contrast with DNA
Although the focus is DNA, a brief comparison with RNA highlights the structural logic:
- RNA contains ribose (2′‑OH) instead of deoxyribose, making it more chemically reactive and less stable.
- Uracil replaces thymine, pairing with adenine.
- RNA is usually single‑stranded but can fold into intricate secondary and tertiary structures (hairpins, pseudoknots) that enable catalytic (ribozyme) and regulatory functions.
- The 2′‑OH group influences the helix geometry; RNA typically adopts an A‑form helix.

### 8. Functions of DNA Structure
The double‑helical structure directly enables:
- **Replication**: Strand separation provides a template for the synthesis of a new complementary strand.
- **Transcription**: A gene’s sequence is copied into RNA by RNA polymerase.
- **Recombination and repair**: The base‑pairing complementarity guides DNA repair machinery and homologous recombination.

The grooves allow proteins to “read” the sequence without unwinding the helix, a feature central to gene regulation.

## Summary
- DNA and RNA are nucleotide polymers; each nucleotide contains a base, a pentose sugar, and phosphate.
- DNA’s primary structure is a directional sequence of nucleotides linked by 3′–5′ phosphodiester bonds.
- The secondary structure is an antiparallel double helix stabilised by complementary A‑T and G‑C base pairs, as reflected in Chargaff’s rules ($A = T$, $G = C$).
- The B‑form helix has a diameter of ~2 nm, 10 bp per turn, and exhibits major and minor grooves for protein recognition.
- Higher‑order packaging through supercoiling and chromatin formation compacts DNA and controls genetic accessibility.
- Understanding these structural principles is essential for grasping replication, transcription, and the molecular basis of heredity.
