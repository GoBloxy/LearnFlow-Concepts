# Sequences and Series

## Introduction
Sequences and series are foundational concepts in calculus and mathematical analysis. A **sequence** is an ordered list of numbers, while a **series** is the sum of the terms of a sequence. Understanding their behavior—especially convergence—underpins many topics, from function approximation and differential equations to Fourier analysis and complex variables. This article provides a comprehensive overview of sequences, infinite series, convergence tests, power series, and Taylor expansions, with formal definitions, key theorems, and worked examples.

---

## 1. Sequences

### 1.1 Definition and Notation
A **sequence** $\{a_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ is a function whose domain is the set of positive integers. The numbers $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$ are the **terms** of the sequence.

**Examples:**
- $a_n = \frac{1}{n}$ : $1, \tfrac12, \tfrac13, \tfrac14, \dots$
- $a_n = (-1)^n$ : $-1, 1, -1, 1, \dots$
- $a_n = \frac{n}{n+1}$ : $\tfrac12, \tfrac23, \tfrac34, \dots$

### 1.2 Convergence and Limits
A sequence $\{a_n\}$ **converges** to a limit $L$ if for every $\epsilon > 0$ there exists an integer $N$ such that $|a_n - L| < \epsilon$ for all $n > N$. We write $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L$. If no such $L$ exists, the sequence **diverges**.

**Example:**  
Prove $a_n = \frac{3n+1}{2n+5} \to \frac{3}{2}$.  
Given $\epsilon > 0$, choose $N > \frac{13}{4\epsilon} - \frac{5}{2}$. Then $n > N$ implies
$$
\left| \frac{3n+1}{2n+5} - \frac{3}{2} \right| 
= \left| \frac{6n+2 - 6n-15}{2(2n+5)} \right|
= \frac{13}{4n+10} < \epsilon.
$$

**Limit Laws:**  
If $\lim a_n = A$ and $\lim b_n = B$, then  
- $\lim (a_n \pm b_n) = A \pm B$  
- $\lim (c\,a_n) = cA$  
- $\lim (a_n b_n) = AB$  
- $\lim \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \frac{A}{B}$, provided $B \neq 0$.

### 1.3 Properties of Convergent Sequences
- A convergent sequence is **bounded** (there exists $M$ such that $|a_n| \le M$ for all $n$).
- If $a_n \le b_n$ for all $n$ beyond some $N$, then $\lim a_n \le \lim b_n$.
- Every bounded monotonic sequence converges. (A sequence is **increasing** if $a_{n+1} \ge a_n$, **decreasing** if $a_{n+1} \le a_n$, and **monotonic** if it is either.)

### 1.4 The Squeeze Theorem
If $a_n \le b_n \le c_n$ for all large $n$, and $\lim a_n = \lim c_n = L$, then $\lim b_n = L$.

**Example:**  
For $b_n = \frac{\cos n}{n}$, we have $-\frac{1}{n} \le \frac{\cos n}{n} \le \frac{1}{n}$. Since $\pm\frac{1}{n} \to 0$, the squeeze theorem gives $\frac{\cos n}{n} \to 0$.

### 1.5 Common Limits and Divergent Behavior
- $\lim_{n\to\infty} r^n = 0$ if $|r| < 1$; diverges (or oscillates) otherwise.
- $\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n^p} = 0$ for $p > 0$.
- $\lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = e^x$.

## 2. Infinite Series

### 2.1 Definition and Partial Sums
An **infinite series** is the sum of the terms of a sequence:
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n = a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \cdots
$$
Its convergence is determined by the sequence of **partial sums**: $S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n$.  
The series **converges** to $S$ if $\lim_{N\to\infty} S_N = S$. Otherwise it **diverges**.

**Telescoping series**:  
Consider $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n+1)}$. Using partial fractions,
$$
a_n = \frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1}, \quad
S_N = 1 - \frac{1}{N+1} \to 1.
$$
So the series converges to $1$.

### 2.2 Geometric Series
A geometric series has the form $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n$, with $a \neq 0$. Its partial sum is
$$
S_N = a\frac{1-r^{N+1}}{1-r} \quad (r \neq 1).
$$
Thus the series **converges** if $|r| < 1$:
$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r}.
$$
If $|r| \ge 1$, the series diverges.

**Example:**  
$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 5\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n = \frac{5}{1-2/3} = 15$.

### 2.3 Divergence Test
If $\sum a_n$ converges, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = 0$. Equivalently, if $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n \neq 0$ or does not exist, the series diverges.

**Warning:** The converse is false. The harmonic series $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ has $a_n \to 0$ yet diverges.

### 2.4 Harmonic Series and $p$-Series
- **Harmonic series:** $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}$ diverges (integral test or grouping).
- **$p$-series:** $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^p}$ converges if $p > 1$, diverges if $p \le 1$.

## 3. Convergence Tests for Series of Positive Terms

Assume $a_n \ge 0$ for all $n$.

### 3.1 The Integral Test
If $f(x)$ is continuous, positive, and decreasing on $[1,\infty)$, and $a_n = f(n)$, then  
$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ converges $\iff$ $\int_{1}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ converges.

**Example:**  
For $f(x)=\frac{1}{x^2}$, $\int_1^\infty \frac{dx}{x^2} = 1$, so the $p$-series with $p=2$ converges.

### 3.2 Comparison Test
If $0 \le a_n \le b_n$ for all sufficiently large $n$:
- $\sum b_n$ converges $\implies$ $\sum a_n$ converges.
- $\sum a_n$ diverges $\implies$ $\sum b_n$ diverges.

**Limit Comparison Test:**  
If $\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = c$, with $0 < c < \infty$, then both series either converge or diverge.

**Example:**  
Test $\sum \frac{n}{n^3+1}$. Compare with $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$: $\frac{n/(n^3+1)}{1/n^2} = \frac{n^3}{n^3+1} \to 1$. Since $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges, the given series converges.

### 3.3 Ratio Test
Let $\rho = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right|$.
- If $\rho < 1$, the series converges absolutely.
- If $\rho > 1$ or $\rho = \infty$, the series diverges.
- If $\rho = 1$, the test is inconclusive.

**Example:**  
$\sum \frac{3^n}{n!}$. Then $\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \frac{3}{n+1} \to 0 < 1$, so converges absolutely.

### 3.4 Root Test
Let $\rho = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$.
- $\rho < 1$ $\implies$ absolute convergence.
- $\rho > 1$ $\implies$ divergence.
- $\rho = 1$ inconclusive.

**Example:**  
$\sum \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^{n^2}$. Then $\sqrt[n]{a_n} = \left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^n \to e > 1$, series diverges.

## 4. Alternating Series and Absolute Convergence

### 4.1 Alternating Series Test
An alternating series $\sum (-1)^{n-1} b_n$ (with $b_n > 0$) converges if:
1. $b_{n+1} \le b_n$ for all large $n$ (decreasing),  
2. $\lim_{n\to\infty} b_n = 0$.

**Example:**  
Alternating harmonic series $\sum (-1)^{n-1} \frac{1}{n}$ converges by this test (though conditionally, see below).

### 4.2 Absolute and Conditional Convergence
- $\sum a_n$ converges **absolutely** if $\sum |a_n|$ converges.
- If $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum |a_n|$ diverges, the series is **conditionally convergent**.

**Absolute convergence implies convergence**, but not vice versa.  
The alternating harmonic series is conditionally convergent: $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges, but $\sum (-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}$ converges.  
Conditionally convergent series can be rearranged to sum to any real number (Riemann rearrangement theorem).

**Example:**  
$\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ converges absolutely because $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ is a convergent $p$-series.

## 5. Power Series

### 5.1 Definition
A **power series** centered at $x = a$ is an expression
$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (x-a)^n,
$$
where $c_n$ are constants. It can be thought of as an infinite polynomial.

### 5.2 Radius and Interval of Convergence
For a given power series, there exists a number $R \ge 0$ (the **radius of convergence**) such that the series converges absolutely for $|x-a| < R$, diverges for $|x-a| > R$, and may converge or diverge at the endpoints $x = a \pm R$. The set of all $x$ where it converges is the **interval of convergence**.

**Finding $R$:** Often use Ratio or Root Test.  
For $\sum c_n (x-a)^n$, compute
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} \left| \frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n} \right| = L \quad \Rightarrow \quad R = \frac{1}{L} \quad (\text{with } R=\infty \text{ if } L=0,\; R=0 \text{ if } L=\infty).
$$

**Example:**  
$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(x-2)^n}{n\, 3^n}$.  
Ratio: $\left| \frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n} \right| = \frac{1}{3} \frac{n}{n+1} \to \frac{1}{3}$, so $R = 3$.  
Center $a=2$, interval $|x-2|<3$: $(-1,5)$. Test endpoints separately:  
$x=-1$ gives $\sum \frac{(-3)^n}{n\,3^n} = \sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ converges (alternating harmonic);  
$x=5$ gives $\sum \frac{3^n}{n\,3^n} = \sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges.  
Interval: $[-1,5)$.

### 5.3 Differentiation and Integration of Power Series
Within its interval of convergence, a power series can be differentiated and integrated term by term:
$$
\frac{d}{dx}\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n (x-a)^n = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n c_n (x-a)^{n-1},
$$
$$
\int \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n (x-a)^n\,dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{c_n}{n+1} (x-a)^{n+1}.
$$
Both resulting series have the same radius of convergence $R$ (but endpoint behavior may change).

## 6. Taylor and Maclaurin Series

### 6.1 Taylor’s Theorem
If $f$ is infinitely differentiable at $a$, its **Taylor series** is
$$
f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (x-a)^n.
$$
The special case $a=0$ is the **Maclaurin series**: $\displaystyle f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!} x^n$.

Taylor’s formula with remainder: $f(x) = P_N(x) + R_N(x)$, where $P_N$ is the $N$th-degree Taylor polynomial and $R_N(x)$ is the remainder. Often $R_N(x) \to 0$ as $N\to\infty$ for $x$ in the interval of convergence, and the series equals $f(x)$.
