# Vectors and the Geometry of Space

## Introduction
Vectors provide a powerful language for describing the geometry of two- and three-dimensional space. They allow us to represent points, directions, forces, and velocities in a coordinate-free manner, and their algebraic operationsâ€”addition, scalar multiplication, dot product, and cross productâ€”translate geometric ideas such as length, angle, area, and projection into precise computations. This article develops the essential vector algebra and applies it to lines, planes, curves, and surfaces in three-dimensional space. Along the way we illustrate each concept with concrete examples to bridge the gap between abstract definitions and geometric intuition.

---

## 1. Vectors in the Plane and Space

A **vector** is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Geometrically, we visualize a vector as a directed line segment. In a Cartesian coordinate system, a vector $\vec{v}$ in the plane can be written as an ordered pair $\langle v_1, v_2 \rangle$; in space it is an ordered triple $\langle v_1, v_2, v_3 \rangle$. The numbers $v_i$ are called the **components** of the vector.

The **magnitude** (or length) of $\vec{v}$ is
$$
\|\vec{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2} \quad\text{(in $\mathbb{R}^2$)}, \qquad
\|\vec{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2} \quad\text{(in $\mathbb{R}^3$)}.
$$

A vector of length $1$ is a **unit vector**. Any nonzero vector $\vec{v}$ can be normalized:
$$
\hat{v} = \frac{\vec{v}}{\|\vec{v}\|}.
$$

The standard basis vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ are $\mathbf{i} = \langle 1,0 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{j} = \langle 0,1 \rangle$. In $\mathbb{R}^3$ we add $\mathbf{k} = \langle 0,0,1 \rangle$, so any vector $\vec{v} = \langle v_1, v_2, v_3 \rangle$ can be expressed as
$$
\vec{v} = v_1\mathbf{i} + v_2\mathbf{j} + v_3\mathbf{k}.
$$

The vector from point $P(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ to point $Q(x_2,y_2,z_2)$ is
$$
\overrightarrow{PQ} = \langle x_2-x_1,\; y_2-y_1,\; z_2-z_1 \rangle.
$$

**Example 1.1**  
Find the unit vector in the direction of $\vec{v} = \langle 2, -1, 2 \rangle$.

$\|\vec{v}\| = \sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{4+1+4} = 3$, so $\hat{v} = \big\langle \frac{2}{3}, -\frac{1}{3}, \frac{2}{3} \big\rangle$.

## 2. Vector Operations

Vector addition and scalar multiplication are defined componentwise. For $\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle$, $\vec{b} = \langle b_1,b_2,b_3 \rangle$, and scalar $c$:
$$
\vec{a} + \vec{b} = \langle a_1+b_1,\; a_2+b_2,\; a_3+b_3 \rangle, \qquad
c\,\vec{a} = \langle ca_1, ca_2, ca_3 \rangle.
$$

Geometrically, addition corresponds to the triangle or parallelogram law, and scalar multiplication stretches or compresses the vector (reversing direction if $c<0$).

Properties: commutative, associative, identity ($\vec{0}$), inverses; distributive laws hold.

**Example 2.1**  
Let $\vec{u} = \langle 1,0,-3 \rangle$, $\vec{v} = \langle -2,4,1 \rangle$. Then $2\vec{u} - \vec{v} = \langle 2,0,-6 \rangle - \langle -2,4,1 \rangle = \langle 4, -4, -7 \rangle$.

## 3. The Dot Product

The **dot product** (or scalar product) of two vectors $\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle$ and $\vec{b} = \langle b_1,b_2,b_3 \rangle$ is
$$
\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3.
$$
The result is a scalar, not a vector.

The geometric interpretation is
$$
\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\|\,\|\vec{b}\|\,\cos\theta,
$$
where $\theta$ ($0 \le \theta \le \pi$) is the angle between the vectors when they are placed tail to tail. This yields a test for orthogonality: $\vec{a} \perp \vec{b}$ iff $\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} = 0$ (provided neither is zero).

**Projection.** The scalar projection of $\vec{b}$ onto $\vec{a}$ is $\operatorname{comp}_{\vec{a}}\vec{b} = \frac{\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|}$, and the vector projection is $\operatorname{proj}_{\vec{a}}\vec{b} = \left(\frac{\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|^2}\right)\vec{a}$.

**Work.** In physics, the work done by a constant force $\vec{F}$ moving an object along displacement $\vec{d}$ is $W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}$.

**Example 3.1**  
Find the angle between $\vec{a} = \langle 1,2,2 \rangle$ and $\vec{b} = \langle -2,1,0 \rangle$.

$\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} = (1)(-2)+(2)(1)+(2)(0) = 0$, so $\cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/2$; the vectors are perpendicular.

**Example 3.2**  
Find the projection of $\vec{b} = \langle 3,1,-1 \rangle$ onto $\vec{a} = \langle 2,2,1 \rangle$.

$\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} = 6+2-1 = 7$, $\|\vec{a}\|^2 = 4+4+1=9$. So $\operatorname{proj}_{\vec{a}}\vec{b} = \frac{7}{9}\langle 2,2,1 \rangle = \big\langle \frac{14}{9}, \frac{14}{9}, \frac{7}{9} \big\rangle$.

## 4. The Cross Product

The **cross product** (or vector product) is defined only in $\mathbb{R}^3$. For $\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle$, $\vec{b} = \langle b_1,b_2,b_3 \rangle$,
$$
\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \langle a_2b_3 - a_3b_2,\; a_3b_1 - a_1b_3,\; a_1b_2 - a_2b_1 \rangle.
$$
A convenient mnemonic uses a $3\times3$ determinant:
$$
\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\
b_1 & b_2 & b_3
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

**Geometric meaning:** $\vec{a} \times \vec{b}$ is a vector perpendicular to both $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$, with direction given by the **right-hand rule**. Its magnitude is
$$
\|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\| = \|\vec{a}\|\,\|\vec{b}\|\,\sin\theta,
$$
which equals the **area of the parallelogram** spanned by $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$. The area of the triangle formed by these vectors is $\frac{1}{2}\|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\|$.

**Torque.** The torque $\vec{\tau}$ about a point produced by a force $\vec{F}$ applied at position $\vec{r}$ is $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$.

Important algebraic properties: anti-commutative ($\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = -\vec{b} \times \vec{a}$), distributive, and scalar multiplication passes through. The cross product of parallel vectors is $\vec{0}$.

**Example 4.1**  
Find a vector perpendicular to both $\vec{u} = \langle 2,1,-3 \rangle$ and $\vec{v} = \langle 0,-1,2 \rangle$.

$\vec{u} \times \vec{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 2 & 1 & -3 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{i}(1\cdot2 - (-3)(-1)) - \mathbf{j}(2\cdot2 - (-3)(0)) + \mathbf{k}(2(-1) - 1\cdot0) = \langle 2-3, -(4-0), -2 \rangle = \langle -1, -4, -2 \rangle.$ This (or any scalar multiple) is perpendicular to both.

**Example 4.2**  
Find the area of the parallelogram with vertices $A(1,0,0), B(0,2,0), C(-1,0,0), D(0,-2,0)$ (actually a rhombus). Vectors $\vec{AB} = \langle -1,2,0 \rangle$, $\vec{AD} = \langle -1,-2,0 \rangle$. $\vec{AB}\times\vec{AD} = \langle 0,0, ( -1)(-2)-2(-1) \rangle = \langle 0,0,2+2 \rangle = \langle 0,0,4 \rangle$, area = $4$.

## 5. Lines and Planes in Space

### Lines
A line $L$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is determined by a point $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ on it and a direction vector $\vec{v} = \langle a,b,c \rangle$ (nonzero). The **vector equation** is
$$
\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_0 + t\,\vec{v}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R},
$$
where $\vec{r}_0 = \langle x_0,y_0,z_0 \rangle$. Componentwise, we obtain **parametric equations**:
$$
x = x_0 + at,\quad y = y_0 + bt,\quad z = z_0 + ct.
$$
If $a,b,c$ are all nonzero, we can eliminate $t$ to get **symmetric equations**:
$$
\frac{x-x_0}{a} = \frac{y-y_0}{b} = \frac{z-z_0}{c}.
$$

### Planes
A plane is determined by a point $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ and a **normal vector** $\vec{n} = \langle A,B,C \rangle$ perpendicular to the plane. The standard scalar equation is
$$
A(x-x_0) + B(y-y_0) + C(z-z_0) = 0,
$$
or $Ax + By + Cz = D$, where $D = Ax_0+By_0+Cz_0$.

The distance from a point $P_1(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ to the plane $Ax + By + Cz = D$ is
$$
d = \frac{|Ax_1 + By_1 + Cz_1 - D|}{\sqrt{A^2+B^2+C^2}}.
$$

**Example 5.1 (Line through two points)**  
Find parametric equations for the line through $P(1,2,-1)$ and $Q(3,0,4)$.

Direction vector $\vec{PQ} = \langle 2, -2, 5 \rangle$. So $x=1+2t$, $y=2-2t$, $z=-1+5t$.

**Example 5.2 (Plane through three points)**  
Find the equation of the plane containing $P(1,0,1)$, $Q(2,-1,0)$, $R(0,3,2)$.

Vectors $\vec{PQ} = \langle 1,-1,-1 \rangle$, $\vec{PR} = \langle -1,3,1 \rangle$. A normal vector:
$\vec{n} = \vec{PQ} \times \vec{PR} = \langle (-1)(1)-(-1)(3),\; (-1)(-1)-1(1),\; 1\cdot3-(-1)(-1) \rangle = \langle -1+3,\; 1-1,\; 3-1 \rangle = \langle 2,0,2 \rangle$, i.e., $\langle 1,0,1 \rangle$ (simplified). Using point $P$: $1(x-1)+0(y-0)+1(z-1)=0 \Rightarrow x + z = 2$.

## 6. Vector Functions and Space Curves

A **vector function** maps a real variable $t$ to a vector: $\vec{r}(t) = \langle f(t), g(t), h(t) \rangle$. It describes a curve in space parametrized by $t$. The **derivative** is defined componentwise:
$$
\vec{r}'(t) = \langle f'(t), g'(t), h'(t) \rangle,
$$
and gives the **tangent vector** to the curve at $\vec{r}(t)$, provided $\vec{r}'(t) \neq \vec{0}$. The unit tangent vector is $\vec{T}(t) = \frac{\vec{r}'(t)}{\|\vec{r}'(t)\|}$.

The **arc length** of the curve from $t=a$ to $t=b$ is
$$
s = \int_a^b \|\vec{r}'(t)\| \, dt.
$$
The **curvature** $\kappa$ measures how fast the curve bends:
$$
\kappa(t) = \frac{\|\vec{T}'(t)\|}{\|\vec{r}'(t)\|} = \frac{\|\vec{r}'(t) \times \vec{r}''(t)\|}{\|\vec{r}'(t)\|^3}.
$$
