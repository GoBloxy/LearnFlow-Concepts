# Partial Derivatives

## Introduction

In single-variable calculus, the derivative measures the instantaneous rate of change of a function with respect to its independent variable. When we move to functions of several variables, the situation becomes richer: a function $f(x,y)$ can change at different rates depending on the direction we move in its domain. The **partial derivative** captures the rate of change of a multivariable function along one of its coordinate axes, while all other variables are held constant.

For example, the temperature on a metal plate depends on the position $(x,y)$. The rate at which temperature changes as you move strictly east (increasing $x$) is a partial derivative with respect to $x$; moving strictly north gives the partial derivative with respect to $y$. This article develops the definition, notation, computation, geometric meaning, and higher-order partial derivatives, providing a foundation for vector calculus and optimization.

## Core Concepts

### Definition of a Partial Derivative

Let $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function of $n$ variables. The **partial derivative** of $f$ with respect to the variable $x_i$ at a point $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \dots, a_n)$ is defined by the limit

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a_1, \dots, a_{i-1}, a_i + h, a_{i+1}, \dots, a_n) - f(a_1, \dots, a_n)}{h},
$$

provided the limit exists. This is exactly the ordinary derivative of the single-variable function obtained by fixing all variables except $x_i$. For a function of two variables, $f(x,y)$, we have two partial derivatives:

$$
f_x(x,y) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h, y) - f(x,y)}{h},
\qquad
f_y(x,y) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x, y+h) - f(x,y)}{h}.
$$

### Notation

Several equivalent notations are used:

- $\displaystyle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ (Leibniz)
- $f_x$, $f_y$ (subscript)
- $D_x f$, $D_y f$ (operator style)

The symbol $\partial$ (often read “del” or “partial”) distinguishes the partial derivative from the ordinary derivative $d/dx$.

### Geometric Interpretation

For a function $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, the graph $z = f(x,y)$ is a surface in three-dimensional space. At a point $(x_0, y_0)$, the intersection of this surface with the vertical plane $y = y_0$ gives a curve. The slope of the tangent line to this curve at $(x_0, y_0, f(x_0,y_0))$ is exactly the partial derivative $f_x(x_0,y_0)$. Similarly, slicing with $x = x_0$ yields a curve whose tangent slope is $f_y(x_0,y_0)$. Thus, $f_x$ and $f_y$ represent the slopes of the surface in the directions of the $x$- and $y$-axes, respectively.

### Computing Partial Derivatives

To compute $\partial f / \partial x$, treat all variables other than $x$ as constants and differentiate with respect to $x$ using the ordinary rules (power, product, chain, etc.). The same principle applies to $\partial f / \partial y$. This simplicity is one of the great strengths of partial differentiation.

**Example 1**: Find $f_x$ and $f_y$ for $f(x,y) = x^3 y^2 + e^{x} \sin y$.

- For $f_x$, treat $y$ as constant:
  $$
  \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^3 y^2) = 3x^2 y^2,
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(e^{x} \sin y) = e^{x} \sin y,
  $$
  so $f_x = 3x^2 y^2 + e^x \sin y$.
- For $f_y$, treat $x$ as constant:
  $$
  \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^3 y^2) = 2x^3 y,
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(e^{x} \sin y) = e^{x} \cos y,
  $$
  hence $f_y = 2x^3 y + e^x \cos y$.

**Example 2**: $f(x,y) = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. For $(x,y) \neq (0,0)$,
$$
f_x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}}, \quad f_y = \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}}.
$$
At $(0,0)$ the limits do not exist; the surface $z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ (a cone) has a cusp at the origin and no well-defined tangent plane.

### Higher‑Order Partial Derivatives

Since the first partial derivatives $f_x$ and $f_y$ are themselves functions of $x$ and $y$, we can differentiate them again to obtain **second‑order partial derivatives**. There are four possibilities for a function of two variables:

- $\displaystyle \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) = f_{xx}$,
- $\displaystyle \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) = f_{xy}$,
- $\displaystyle \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) = f_{yx}$,
- $\displaystyle \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) = f_{yy}$.

Mixed partials like $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are typically computed by first taking the derivative with respect to one variable, then the other. The order of the subscripts indicates the order of differentiation; e.g., $f_{xy} = (f_x)_y$.

**Clairaut’s Theorem** (also called the equality of mixed partials) states that if $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are continuous on an open region, then they are equal at every point of that region: $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$. This permits us to choose the more convenient order of differentiation when computing mixed partials.

**Example of higher-order derivatives**: Let $f(x,y) = x^3 y^2 + e^x y$.

First partials:
$$
f_x = 3x^2 y^2 + e^x y, \qquad f_y = 2x^3 y + e^x.
$$
Second partials:
$$
f_{xx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3x^2 y^2 + e^x y) = 6x y^2 + e^x y,
$$
$$
f_{yy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2x^3 y + e^x) = 2x^3,
$$
$$
f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(f_x) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3x^2 y^2 + e^x y) = 6x^2 y + e^x,
$$
$$
f_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(f_y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2x^3 y + e^x) = 6x^2 y + e^x.
$$
Indeed, $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$, as guaranteed by Clairaut’s theorem.

Higher orders are defined analogously; for example $f_{xxy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(f_{xx})$.

### Partial Derivatives and Differentiability

Existence of partial derivatives at a point does **not** guarantee that the function is continuous, or even differentiable, there. Consider

$$
f(x,y) = \begin{cases}
\dfrac{xy}{x^2 + y^2}, & (x,y) \neq (0,0),\\
0, & (x,y) = (0,0).
\end{cases}
$$

Both $f_x(0,0)$ and $f_y(0,0)$ exist (they equal 0), but $f$ is not continuous at the origin. For a function to be **differentiable** in the sense of having a good linear approximation, the partial derivatives must not only exist but also be continuous near the point. Differentiability is a stronger condition that will lead to the concept of the total derivative and the gradient.

### Connections to the Gradient and Directional Derivatives

The vector of first partial derivatives,
$$
\nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \dots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right),
$$
is the **gradient** of $f$. It points in the direction of steepest ascent, and its magnitude gives that maximal rate of change. The **directional derivative** in the direction of a unit vector $\mathbf{u}$ is the dot product $\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$. The partial derivatives are precisely the directional derivatives along the standard basis vectors $\mathbf{e}_i$.

## Summary

- **Partial derivative** $\partial f/\partial x_i$ measures how a multivariable function changes as $x_i$ varies while all other variables stay fixed.
- It is defined by a limit analogous to the ordinary derivative, and computed by treating other variables as constants.
- Geometrically, for $f(x,y)$, $f_x$ and $f_y$ give the slopes of the surface $z = f(x,y)$ in the $x$ and $y$ directions.
- **Higher-order partial derivatives** are obtained by repeated partial differentiation. Under mild continuity conditions, mixed partials are equal (Clairaut’s theorem).
- Existence of partial derivatives does not alone imply continuity or differentiability; stronger conditions are needed for a good linear approximation.
- Partial derivatives are the building blocks of the gradient, directional derivatives, and later of optimization and multiple integration.

Mastering partial derivatives allows you to analyze functions of several variables and opens the door to advanced topics such as multivariable calculus, physics, engineering, and data science.
