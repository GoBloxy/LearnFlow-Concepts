# Limits and Continuity

## Introduction
In calculus, the notions of **limit** and **continuity** form the foundation upon which differentiation and integration are built. A limit describes the behavior of a function as its input approaches a particular value, while continuity captures the idea of a function having no breaks, jumps, or holes. Mastering these concepts is essential for understanding rates of change, accumulation, and the deeper properties of functions.

This article provides a rigorous yet accessible treatment of limits and continuity at the undergraduate level, with clear definitions, fundamental theorems, and worked examples.

## The Concept of a Limit

### Intuitive Idea
Suppose a function $f(x)$ is defined for all $x$ near a point $a$, except possibly at $a$ itself. We say
\[
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L
\]
if the values of $f(x)$ can be made arbitrarily close to $L$ by taking $x$ sufficiently close to $a$ (but not equal to $a$). The limit describes the *target* value, not necessarily the function’s value at $a$.

### Formal Definition (ε–δ)
For completeness, the precise definition:  
$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ if for every $\epsilon > 0$ there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that whenever $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, we have $|f(x) - L| < \epsilon$. This article focuses on computational methods and conceptual understanding; the formal definition is rarely used directly for elementary evaluations.

### Basic Limit Laws
If $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x)$ exist, then:
- $\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \pm g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \pm \lim_{x \to a} g(x)$
- $\lim_{x \to a} [c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot \lim_{x \to a} f(x)$
- $\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a} g(x)$
- $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{\lim_{x \to a} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to a} g(x)}$, provided $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) \neq 0$.

### Example 1: Direct Substitution
Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 2} (3x^2 - 4x + 1)$.

Since the polynomial is continuous everywhere (as we will formalize later), we can simply plug in $x=2$:
\[
\lim_{x \to 2} (3x^2 - 4x + 1) = 3(2)^2 - 4(2) + 1 = 12 - 8 + 1 = 5.
\]

### Example 2: Removing an Indeterminate Form
Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

Direct substitution yields $\frac{0}{0}$, an indeterminate form. Factor the numerator:
\[
\frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3}.
\]
For $x \neq 3$, we can cancel the common factor, leaving $x+3$. Thus,
\[
\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \lim_{x \to 3} (x+3) = 6.
\]

### The Squeeze Theorem
If $g(x) \leq f(x) \leq h(x)$ for all $x$ near $a$ (except possibly at $a$), and
\[
\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} h(x) = L,
\]
then $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$.

**Example 3:** Show $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = 0$.

We know $-1 \leq \sin(1/x) \leq 1$, so
\[
- x^2 \leq x^2 \sin(1/x) \leq x^2.
\]
Since $\lim_{x \to 0} (-x^2) = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 = 0$, the Squeeze Theorem gives the desired limit $0$.

## One-Sided Limits
Sometimes the behavior of a function differs as $x$ approaches $a$ from the left or from the right.

- **Left-hand limit:** $\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L$ means $f(x) \to L$ as $x \to a$ with $x < a$.
- **Right-hand limit:** $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$ means $f(x) \to L$ as $x \to a$ with $x > a$.

The two-sided limit $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists **if and only if** both one-sided limits exist and are equal:
\[
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L.
\]

### Example 4: Piecewise Function
Define
\[
f(x) = 
\begin{cases}
x^2 + 1, & x < 1, \\
3 - x, & x > 1.
\end{cases}
\]
Find $\lim_{x \to 1} f(x)$ if it exists.

Compute one-sided limits:
\[
\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 1^-} (x^2+1) = 2, \qquad
\lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 1^+} (3-x) = 2.
\]
Both equal $2$, so $\lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 2$, regardless of the fact that $f(1)$ may not even be defined.

## Infinite Limits and Limits at Infinity

### Infinite Limits (Vertical Asymptotes)
If $f(x)$ grows without bound as $x \to a$, we write
\[
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty \quad \text{(or } -\infty\text{)}.
\]
This indicates a **vertical asymptote** at $x = a$.

**Example 5:** $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} = \infty$, and $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x} = -\infty$. Since the one-sided limits are not equal (and not finite), $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x}$ does not exist.

### Limits at Infinity (Horizontal Asymptotes)
We examine the long-term behavior of $f(x)$ as $x \to \infty$ or $x \to -\infty$. If the limit exists and equals $L$, the line $y = L$ is a **horizontal asymptote**.

**Example 6:** $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$, so $y = 0$ is a horizontal asymptote.

For rational functions, divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of $x$ in the denominator.

**Example 7:** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x^2 - 3x + 1}{x^2 + 4}$.

Divide each term by $x^2$:
\[
\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2 - \frac{3}{x} + \frac{1}{x^2}}{1 + \frac{4}{x^2}} 
= \frac{2 - 0 + 0}{1 + 0} = 2.
\]
So $y = 2$ is a horizontal asymptote.

## Continuity

### Definition
A function $f$ is **continuous at a point** $a$ if three conditions hold:
1. $f(a)$ is defined,
2. $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists,
3. $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$.

If $f$ is continuous at every point in an interval, we say $f$ is **continuous on that interval**.

Geometrically, a continuous function can be drawn without lifting the pen.

### Common Continuous Functions
- Polynomials: continuous for all real numbers.
- Rational functions: continuous wherever the denominator is nonzero.
- Trigonometric functions: $\sin x$, $\cos x$ continuous everywhere; $\tan x$ continuous except at $x = \frac{\pi}{2} + k\pi$.
- Exponential and logarithmic functions: $e^x$ continuous everywhere; $\ln x$ continuous for $x > 0$.
- Root functions: $\sqrt{x}$ continuous on $[0, \infty)$.

### Example 8: Checking Continuity
Show $f(x) = \sqrt{x-2}$ is continuous at $x = 6$.

- $f(6) = \sqrt{4} = 2$ is defined.
- $\lim_{x \to 6} \sqrt{x-2} = \sqrt{6-2} = 2$ (by limit laws and continuity of the square root).
- The limit equals the function value, so $f$ is continuous at $x=6$.

### Example 9: Discontinuity from a Hole
Let $g(x) = \frac{x^2-4}{x-2}$ for $x \neq 2$, and define $g(2) = 3$. Is $g$ continuous at $x=2$?

We have $\lim_{x \to 2} g(x) = \lim_{x \to 2} (x+2) = 4$ (after factoring). But $g(2) = 3 \neq 4$, so the function is **discontinuous** at $x=2$ despite the limit existing. This is a **removable discontinuity** (a hole) that could be fixed by redefining $g(2)=4$.

## Types of Discontinuities
1. **Removable discontinuity:** $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists but is not equal to $f(a)$, or $f(a)$ is undefined. The graph has a hole that can be “filled” by redefining the function at $a$.
2. **Jump discontinuity:** The left‑ and right‑hand limits exist but are not equal. Example: the greatest integer function $f(x) = \lfloor x \rfloor$ at integer values.
3. **Infinite discontinuity:** At least one of the one‑sided limits is $\infty$ or $-\infty$, indicating a vertical asymptote. Example: $f(x) = 1/x$ at $x = 0$.

## The Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)
If $f$ is continuous on a closed interval $[a, b]$ and $N$ is any number between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ (inclusive), then there exists at least one $c$ in $[a, b]$ such that $f(c) = N$.

The IVT is a powerful tool for proving the existence of solutions to equations.

### Example 10: Proving a Root Exists
Show that the equation $x^3 - x - 1 = 0$ has a root between $1$ and $2$.

Let $f(x) = x^3 - x - 1$, a polynomial and therefore continuous on $[1, 2]$.
\[
f(1) = 1 - 1 - 1 = -1, \qquad f(2) = 8 - 2 - 1 = 5.
\]
Since $f(1) < 0 < f(2)$, the IVT guarantees some $c \in (1,2)$ with $f(c) = 0$, i.e., a root of the equation.

## Summary
- The **limit** of a function at a point describes the value the function approaches. It does not depend on the function’s actual value at that point.
- **One-sided limits** refine the idea of a limit from strictly one direction, and a two-sided limit exists only when both one-sided limits agree.
- **Infinite limits** indicate unbounded behavior (vertical asymptotes), while **limits at infinity** describe end behavior (horizontal asymptotes).
- A function is **continuous** at a point if the limit equals the function’s value. Continuity on an interval guarantees no breaks or jumps.
- Discontinuities are classified as **removable**, **jump**, or **infinite**.
- The **Intermediate Value Theorem** provides a guarantee that continuous functions attain all intermediate values, a key result for existence proofs.

A solid grasp of limits and continuity opens the door to differentiation, where the limit of a difference quotient defines the derivative, and to integration, where limits of Riemann sums define the integral. These concepts remain central throughout all of calculus and real analysis.
