# Calculus

## Introduction

Calculus is the branch of mathematics that studies continuous change. Developed independently by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in the 17th century, it provides the language and tools to model motion, growth, optimization, and the accumulation of quantities. At its heart, calculus rests on two complementary processes: differentiation, which measures instantaneous rates of change, and integration, which aggregates infinitesimal contributions to find totals. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus reveals that these two operations are inverses of one another. This article presents the core undergraduate-level concepts of single‑variable calculus, with a brief outlook on multivariable extensions.

## 1. Limits and Continuity

### 1.1 The Concept of a Limit

The limit describes the value a function $f(x)$ approaches as the input $x$ approaches some point $a$. Notation:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L
$$

means that $f(x)$ can be made arbitrarily close to $L$ by taking $x$ sufficiently close to $a$ (but $x \neq a$). Limits are the foundation upon which both derivatives and integrals are built.

**Example 1.1**  
Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$.

Direct substitution gives $\frac{0}{0}$, an indeterminate form. Factor the numerator:

$$
\frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x+2 \quad (x \neq 2).
$$

Thus, $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \lim_{x \to 2} (x+2) = 4$.

### 1.2 Continuity

A function $f$ is **continuous at $x = a$** if  

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a).
$$

Intuitively, you can draw the graph without lifting your pen. All polynomials, sine, cosine, exponential functions, and roots are continuous on their domains. Discontinuities arise from jumps, vertical asymptotes, or removable holes.

## 2. Differentiation

### 2.1 Definition of the Derivative

The **derivative** of $f$ at $x$ is the limit of the difference quotient:

$$
f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h},
$$

provided the limit exists. Geometrically, $f'(x)$ is the slope of the tangent line to the graph $y = f(x)$ at the point $(x, f(x))$. Leibniz’s notation $\frac{df}{dx}$ or $\frac{dy}{dx}$ is also common.

**Example 2.1**  
Find the derivative of $f(x) = x^2$ from the definition.

$$
f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h}
        = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h}
        = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h}
        = \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) = 2x.
$$

### 2.2 Basic Differentiation Rules

- **Constant rule:** $\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0$.
- **Power rule:** $\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1}$ for any real $n$.
- **Sum rule:** $\frac{d}{dx}[f \pm g] = f' \pm g'$.
- **Product rule:** $\frac{d}{dx}[f \cdot g] = f' g + f g'$.
- **Quotient rule:** $\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{f}{g}\right] = \frac{f' g - f g'}{g^2}$.
- **Chain rule:** If $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$, or $(f \circ g)'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$.

**Example 2.2**  
Differentiate $h(x) = \sin(x^2)$.

Let $u = x^2$, then $h = \sin u$. $\frac{d}{du}[\sin u] = \cos u$, $\frac{du}{dx} = 2x$. By the chain rule,

$$
h'(x) = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x = 2x \cos(x^2).
$$

### 2.3 Derivatives of Elementary Functions

| $f(x)$               | $f'(x)$                     |
|----------------------|-----------------------------|
| $\sin x$             | $\cos x$                    |
| $\cos x$             | $-\sin x$                   |
| $\tan x$             | $\sec^2 x$                  |
| $e^x$                | $e^x$                       |
| $\ln x$              | $\frac{1}{x}$ $(x>0)$       |
| $a^x \ (a>0)$        | $a^x \ln a$                 |

### 2.4 Higher‑Order Derivatives

The second derivative, $f''(x)$ or $\frac{d^2 f}{dx^2}$, is the derivative of the first derivative. It measures concavity and acceleration. Higher orders follow analogously.

## 3. Applications of Derivatives

### 3.1 Tangent Lines and Linear Approximation

The equation of the tangent line to $y = f(x)$ at $x = a$ is

$$
y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a).
$$

For $x$ near $a$, the tangent line gives a linear approximation: $f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)$.

### 3.2 Optimisation

Critical points occur where $f'(x) = 0$ or $f'(x)$ does not exist. The **first derivative test** examines sign changes of $f'$ to classify local maxima and minima. The **second derivative test** states: if $f'(c)=0$ and $f''(c) > 0$, $f$ has a local minimum at $c$; if $f''(c) < 0$, a local maximum.

**Example 3.1**  
Find the maximum area of a rectangle inscribed under the parabola $y = 12 - x^2$ and above the $x$-axis, with its base on the $x$-axis.

Let the base run from $-x$ to $x$ (width $2x$). Height = $12 - x^2$. Area $A(x) = 2x(12 - x^2) = 24x - 2x^3$, for $0 \le x \le \sqrt{12}$. Then $A'(x) = 24 - 6x^2$. Setting $A'=0$ gives $x^2 = 4 \Rightarrow x = 2$. $A''(x) = -12x$, so $A''(2) = -24 < 0$, confirming a maximum. Maximum area $A(2) = 24(2) - 2(8) = 48 - 16 = 32$.

### 3.3 Related Rates and Motion

If position is $s(t)$, velocity is $v(t) = s'(t)$, acceleration $a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)$. Related rates problems link changing quantities via differentiation with respect to time.

## 4. Integration

### 4.1 The Indefinite Integral (Antiderivative)

A function $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ if $F'(x) = f(x)$. The indefinite integral $\int f(x)\,dx$ denotes the family of all antiderivatives:

$$
\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C,
$$

where $C$ is an arbitrary constant.

Basic formulas:

- $\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$
- $\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C$
- $\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C$
- $\int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C$
- $\int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C$

### 4.2 The Definite Integral and Riemann Sums

The definite integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ represents the signed area between the graph of $f$ and the $x$-axis from $x=a$ to $x=b$. It is defined as a limit of Riemann sums:

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*) \Delta x,
$$

where $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$ and $x_i^*$ is a sample point in the $i$-th subinterval. Properties include linearity, additivity on intervals, and reversal of limits.

### 4.3 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

**Part 1:** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then $F'(x) = f(x)$. Differentiation undoes integration.

**Part 2:** If $F$ is any antiderivative of a continuous $f$ on $[a,b]$, then

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a).
$$

This connects the two central operations of calculus.

**Example 4.1**  
Compute $\int_0^{\pi} \sin x\,dx$.

An antiderivative is $-\cos x$. So

$$
\int_0^{\pi} \sin x\,dx = \big[-\cos x\big]_0^{\pi} = -\cos(\pi) - (-\cos 0) = -(-1) + 1 = 2.
$$

### 4.4 Techniques of Integration

- **Substitution:** derived from the chain rule. If $u = g(x)$, then $\int f(g(x)) g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\,du$.
- **Integration by parts:** from the product rule: $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$.
- **Partial fractions:** rational functions decomposed into simpler fractions.
- **Trigonometric integrals and substitutions:** using identities and substitutions like $x = a\sin\theta$.

**Example 4.2**  
Evaluate $\int x e^x\,dx$ using integration by parts.

Let $u = x \Rightarrow du = dx$, $dv = e^x\,dx \Rightarrow v = e^x$. Then

$$
\int x e^x\,dx = x e^x - \int e^x\,dx = x e^x - e^x + C.
$$

## 5. Sequences and Series (A Glimpse)

An infinite series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ is the limit of its sequence of partial sums. Taylor and Maclaurin series express functions as power series:

$$
f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (x-a)^n.
$$

For $a=0$, we obtain the Maclaurin series: $e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!}$, $\sin x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$, etc. Tests (ratio, integral, comparison) determine convergence.

## 6. Introduction to Multivariable Calculus

Extending calculus to functions of several variables, we study:

- **Partial derivatives:** $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ keeps other variables constant.
- **Gradient:** $\nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \dots \right)$, pointing in the direction of steepest ascent.
- **Multiple integrals:** $\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA$ computes volume under a surface. Iterated integrals (Fubini’s theorem) are used for evaluation.
- **Line and surface integrals:** integrate along curves or over surfaces; Green’s, Stokes’, and divergence theorems unify these with fundamental theorem of calculus.

## Summary

Calculus provides an indispensable framework for understanding and quantifying change. Starting from limits, the derivative captures instantaneous rates of change, enabling optimization, approximation, and analysis of dynamic systems. The integral reconstructs total quantities from rates, measuring area, volume, and accumulated change. The Fundamental Theorem cements the inverse relationship between these two pillars. Mastery of single‑variable calculus and its techniques opens the door to advanced mathematics, physics, engineering, economics, and beyond.
