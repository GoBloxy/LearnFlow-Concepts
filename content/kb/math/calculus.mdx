# Calculus

## Introduction
Calculus is the branch of mathematics that studies continuous change. Developed independently by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in the 17th century, it provides the tools to model dynamic systems, compute areas and volumes, and understand rates of change. At the undergraduate level, calculus divides into two complementary operations: **differentiation** (measuring instantaneous rates of change) and **integration** (accumulating quantities). The surprising link between them—the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus—unifies the subject and underpins much of modern science and engineering.

This article presents the core concepts of single-variable calculus with precise definitions, illustrative examples, and a focus on the logical structure that leads from limits to the Fundamental Theorem.

---

## Core Concepts

### 1. Limits: The Foundation of Calculus
The limit describes the behavior of a function \( f(x) \) as the input \( x \) approaches a particular value, without necessarily reaching it. Limits allow us to define derivatives and integrals rigorously.

**Definition (intuitive).** We write  
\[
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L
\]  
if the values of \( f(x) \) can be made arbitrarily close to \( L \) by taking \( x \) sufficiently close to \( a \) (but \( x \neq a \)).

**Example – Removable discontinuity.**  
Let \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \). For \( x \neq 1 \), \( f(x) = x+1 \). Then  
\[
\lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 1} (x+1) = 2,
\]  
even though \( f(1) \) is undefined. The graph has a hole at \( (1,2) \), but the limit exists.

**One-sided limits.** The right-hand limit \( \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) \) and left-hand limit \( \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \) must both exist and be equal for the two-sided limit to exist.

**Limit laws** (sum, product, quotient, etc.) mirror the algebraic rules for real numbers, provided the component limits exist and denominators do not approach zero.

### 2. The Derivative: Instantaneous Rate of Change
The derivative of a function \( f \) at a point \( x = a \) is the slope of the tangent line to the graph at that point, defined as a limit:

\[
f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h},
\]  
provided this limit exists. If \( f \) has a derivative at every point in an interval, we call it **differentiable** and write the derivative function \( f'(x) \) or \( \frac{dy}{dx} \).

**Geometric interpretation:** The difference quotient \( \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \) is the slope of the secant line through \( (a, f(a)) \) and \( (a+h, f(a+h)) \). As \( h \to 0 \), the secant lines approach the tangent line.

**Example – Power rule.**  
If \( f(x) = x^n \), where \( n \) is a constant, then \( f'(x) = n x^{n-1} \). For instance, for \( f(x) = x^3 \):
\[
f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^3 - x^3}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3}{h} = 3x^2.
\]

**Derivative rules:**
- Sum: \( (f+g)' = f' + g' \)
- Product: \( (fg)' = f'g + fg' \)
- Quotient: \( \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} \)
- Chain rule: \( (f \circ g)'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \)

**Example – Chain rule.**  
Differentiate \( h(x) = \sin(x^2) \). Let \( f(u) = \sin u \), \( g(x) = x^2 \). Then \( f'(u) = \cos u \), \( g'(x) = 2x \). Hence  
\[
h'(x) = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x = 2x \cos(x^2).
\]

**Applications:** Derivatives give velocity as the derivative of position, marginal cost in economics, and enable optimization (finding maxima and minima) by setting \( f'(x) = 0 \).

### 3. The Integral: Accumulated Change
The definite integral represents the signed area under a curve. Given a function \( f \) defined on \( [a,b] \), we partition the interval into \( n \) subintervals of width \( \Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n} \), choose sample points \( x_i^* \) in each subinterval, and form the Riemann sum:

\[
\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \Delta x.
\]

The definite integral is the limit of such Riemann sums as \( n \to \infty \) (if the limit exists and is independent of the choice of sample points):

\[
\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \Delta x.
\]

**Example – Area under a line.**  
Compute \( \int_0^2 x \, dx \) using a right‑endpoint Riemann sum. With \( \Delta x = \frac{2}{n} \) and \( x_i^* = \frac{2i}{n} \), the sum is
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(\frac{2i}{n}\right) \frac{2}{n} = \frac{4}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} i = \frac{4}{n^2} \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} = 2 + \frac{2}{n}.
\]  
Taking \( n \to \infty \) gives \( 2 \). Thus \( \int_0^2 x\, dx = 2 \), matching the area of a triangle.

**Properties of definite integrals:**
- Linearity: \( \int (c f + g) = c\int f + \int g \)
- Additivity over intervals: \( \int_a^c f = \int_a^b f + \int_b^c f \)
- Reversal of limits: \( \int_a^b f = -\int_b^a f \)

**The indefinite integral** (antiderivative) is a function \( F \) such that \( F'(x) = f(x) \), written \( \int f(x)\, dx = F(x) + C \), where \( C \) is an arbitrary constant. Basic antiderivatives include:
\[
\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1), \qquad 
\int e^x\, dx = e^x + C, \qquad 
\int \cos x\, dx = \sin x + C.
\]

### 4. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
The Fundamental Theorem connects differentiation and integration, showing they are inverse processes.

**Part 1 (FTC1).** If \( f \) is continuous on \( [a,b] \), then the function  
\[
F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt
\]  
is differentiable on \( (a,b) \) and \( F'(x) = f(x) \).

**Part 2 (FTC2).** If \( F \) is any antiderivative of \( f \) on \( [a,b] \), then  
\[
\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a).
\]

**Example – Using FTC2.**  
Evaluate \( \int_0^\pi \sin x\, dx \). An antiderivative of \( \sin x \) is \( -\cos x \), so  
\[
\int_0^\pi \sin x\, dx = \bigl[-\cos x\bigr]_0^\pi = (-\cos \pi) - (-\cos 0) = (-(-1)) - (-1) = 1 + 1 = 2.
\]  
The area under one arch of the sine curve is exactly 2.

**Example – FTC1 with a variable upper limit.**  
Let \( g(x) = \int_1^{x} \frac{1}{t}\, dt \) for \( x > 0 \). By FTC1, \( g'(x) = \frac{1}{x} \). This shows that the natural logarithm (often defined this way) differentiates to \( 1/x \).

### 5. Techniques of Integration
While FTC2 provides a straightforward path, many integrals require systematic strategies.

- **Substitution (reverse chain rule):**  
  \( \int f(g(x))\, g'(x)\, dx = \int f(u)\, du \) with \( u = g(x) \).  
  *Example:* \( \int 2x \cos(x^2)\, dx \); let \( u = x^2 \), \( du = 2x\, dx \), giving \( \int \cos u\, du = \sin(x^2) + C \).

- **Integration by parts (reverse product rule):**  
  \( \int u\, dv = uv - \int v\, du \).  
  *Example:* \( \int x e^x\, dx \). Set \( u = x \), \( dv = e^x\, dx \), so \( du = dx \), \( v = e^x \). Then  
  \[
  \int x e^x\, dx = x e^x - \int e^x\, dx = x e^x - e^x + C.
  \]

- **Partial fractions** for rational functions, trigonometric substitution for expressions involving \( \sqrt{a^2 \pm x^2} \), etc., complete the toolbox.

### 6. Applications and Extensions
Calculus immediately applies to many real‑world problems:
- **Physics:** Velocity and acceleration; work as \( \int F(x)\, dx \); center of mass.
- **Engineering:** Rates of change in circuits, fluid flow, structural bending.
- **Economics:** Marginal analysis, consumer/producer surplus as areas between curves.
- **Probability:** Cumulative distribution functions derived from probability densities using integrals.

Beyond single variable, these ideas extend to multi‑variable calculus (partial derivatives, multiple integrals, vector calculus), each built on the same foundational limits.

## Summary
Calculus rests on the limit concept, which gives precise meaning to the derivative and the integral. The derivative measures instantaneous rate of change and is used to find slopes, velocities, and optimal values. The definite integral accumulates quantities over an interval, representing area, total change, or net accumulation. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus reveals that differentiation and integration are inverse operations, allowing us to evaluate definite integrals by finding antiderivatives. With these core ideas and a suite of computational techniques, calculus provides an indispensable language for modelling and solving problems across the sciences. Mastering calculus means understanding not only how to compute derivatives and integrals but also grasping the deep logical connections that make the subject a unified whole.
