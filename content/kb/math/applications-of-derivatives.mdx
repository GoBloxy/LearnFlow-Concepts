# Applications of Derivatives

The derivative is one of the most powerful concepts in calculus. It measures how a function changes as its input changes—providing the instantaneous rate of change and the slope of a tangent line. Beyond these geometric and physical interpretations, derivatives are indispensable tools in a wide range of scientific and engineering problems. This article explores the fundamental undergraduate-level applications of derivatives, including rates of change, tangents and normals, linear approximations, optimization, related rates, curve sketching, and the Mean Value Theorem. Each section is accompanied by clear examples and calculations using the $f'(x)$ notation and $\LaTeX$ math.

---

## 1. Rates of Change

For a function $y = f(x)$, the derivative $f'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx}$ represents the instantaneous rate of change of $y$ with respect to $x$. In physics, if $s(t)$ is the position of an object at time $t$, then:

- Velocity: $v(t) = s'(t) = \frac{ds}{dt}$
- Acceleration: $a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)$

### Example
A particle moves along a line with position $s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$ meters after $t$ seconds.
Find its velocity and acceleration functions, and determine when the particle is at rest.

**Solution**
$v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9$,  
$a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 12$.

The particle is at rest when $v(t) = 0$:
$$3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad t^2 - 4t + 3 = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad (t-1)(t-3)=0.$$
Thus $t = 1$ s and $t = 3$ s.

The sign of $v(t)$ shows the direction of motion; acceleration $a(t)$ indicates whether the particle is speeding up or slowing down.

## 2. Tangents and Normals

The derivative $f'(a)$ gives the slope of the tangent line to the curve $y = f(x)$ at the point $P(a, f(a))$.

- **Tangent line equation**: $y - f(a) = f'(a)(x-a)$.
- **Normal line** (perpendicular to tangent): slope is $-\frac{1}{f'(a)}$ (provided $f'(a) \ne 0$). Equation: $y - f(a) = -\frac{1}{f'(a)}(x-a)$.

### Example
Find the tangent and normal lines to $f(x) = x^2$ at the point where $x = 1$.

**Solution**
$f(1) = 1$, $f'(x) = 2x$, so $f'(1) = 2$.
- Tangent line: $y - 1 = 2(x - 1) \;\Rightarrow\; y = 2x - 1$.
- Normal line: slope $= -\frac12$, so $y - 1 = -\frac12 (x - 1) \;\Rightarrow\; y = -\frac12 x + \frac32$.

These lines provide local linear approximations to the curve.

## 3. Linear Approximations and Differentials

If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then for $x$ near $a$,
$$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a).$$
The right-hand side is the **linearization** $L(x)$ of $f$ at $a$. This is the basis for differentials: $dy = f'(x)\,dx$, where $dx$ is a small change in $x$ and $dy$ approximates the actual change $\Delta y$.

### Example
Use a linear approximation to estimate $\sqrt{4.1}$.

**Solution**
Let $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$, $a = 4$. Then $f(4) = 2$, $f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$, so $f'(4) = \frac{1}{4}$.
$$
L(x) = 2 + \frac14 (x - 4).
$$
For $x = 4.1$:
$$L(4.1) = 2 + \frac14 (0.1) = 2 + 0.025 = 2.025.$$
The true value is $\sqrt{4.1} \approx 2.02485$, so the approximation is excellent.

## 4. Optimization

Derivatives help locate maximum and minimum values of functions—a cornerstone of design, economics, and science.

- **Critical points**: interior points where $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist.
- **First Derivative Test**: examine sign changes of $f'$ around $c$ to classify local maxima/minima.
- **Second Derivative Test**: if $f''(c) < 0$, local maximum; if $f''(c) > 0$, local minimum.
- For a closed interval $[a, b]$, evaluate $f$ at critical points and endpoints to find absolute extrema.

### Example
A farmer has 20 meters of fencing and wants to enclose a rectangular area against a straight wall (so no fencing is needed along the wall). What dimensions maximize the area?

**Solution**
Let the side parallel to the wall be $x$ and each perpendicular side be $y$. Then fencing needed: $x + 2y = 20$, so $x = 20 - 2y$.
Area: $A = x y = (20-2y)y = 20y - 2y^2$, with $0 \le y \le 10$.
$A'(y) = 20 - 4y = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 5$.
$A''(y) = -4 < 0$, so $y = 5$ gives a maximum.
Then $x = 20 - 2(5) = 10$.
Maximum area: $10 \times 5 = 50\, \text{m}^2$.

## 5. Related Rates

In many real-world problems, two or more quantities change with time and are related by an equation. Differentiating implicitly with respect to time $t$ yields a relationship between their rates of change.

### Example
A 10-foot ladder leans against a vertical wall. The bottom slides away from the wall at 2 ft/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the bottom is 6 ft from the wall?

**Solution**
Let $x$ be distance of bottom from wall, $y$ height of top. By Pythagoras: $x^2 + y^2 = 10^2 = 100$.
Differentiate with respect to $t$:
$$2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{x}{y}\,\frac{dx}{dt}.$$
Given $\frac{dx}{dt} = 2$ ft/s, at $x = 6$ ft, we find $y = \sqrt{100 - 36} = 8$ ft.
$$\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{6}{8} \times 2 = -\frac{12}{8} = -1.5 \text{ ft/s}.$$
The negative sign indicates the top is moving downward at 1.5 ft/s.

## 6. Curve Sketching

Derivatives give a complete picture of a function’s behaviour.

- **Increasing / Decreasing**: $f'(x) > 0 \implies$ increasing; $f'(x) < 0 \implies$ decreasing.
- **Concavity**: $f''(x) > 0 \implies$ concave up; $f''(x) < 0 \implies$ concave down.
- **Inflection points**: points where concavity changes and $f''$ changes sign.
- Together with intercepts and asymptotes, one can sketch an accurate graph.

### Example
Sketch the graph of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 2$ using derivative information.

**Solution**
$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 3(x^2 - 1) = 3(x-1)(x+1)$.
Critical points: $x = -1, 1$.
- $f'(x) > 0$ for $x < -1$ and $x > 1$ (increasing).
- $f'(x) < 0$ for $-1 < x < 1$ (decreasing).

$f''(x) = 6x$.
- $f''(x) = 0$ at $x = 0$.
- $f''(x) < 0$ for $x < 0$ (concave down).
- $f''(x) > 0$ for $x > 0$ (concave up).
Thus $x = 0$ is an inflection point.

Values: $f(-1) = -1 + 3 + 2 = 4$ (local max), $f(1) = 1 - 3 + 2 = 0$ (local min), $f(0) = 2$. End behavior: as $x\to\infty, f(x)\to\infty$; as $x\to -\infty, f(x)\to -\infty$.  
The graph crosses $y$-axis at 2 and has the characteristic cubic shape with a max at $(-1,4)$, min at $(1,0)$, and an inflection at $(0,2)$.

## 7. The Mean Value Theorem and Its Uses

**Mean Value Theorem (MVT)**: If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, then there exists a $c \in (a, b)$ such that
$$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}.$$
Geometrically, there is at least one point where the tangent is parallel to the secant joining $(a,f(a))$ and $(b,f(b))$.

Applications:
- Proving that if $f'(x) = 0$ on an interval, then $f$ is constant.
- Establishing inequalities and verifying properties of functions.

### Example
For $f(x) = x^2$ on $[1, 3]$, find the value(s) $c$ guaranteed by the MVT.

**Solution**
$f(1)=1, f(3)=9$, so average rate of change $= \frac{9-1}{3-1} = 4$.
$f'(x) = 2x$, set $2c = 4 \Rightarrow c = 2$ (which lies in $(1,3)$).

## Summary

Derivatives are not merely abstract symbols—they provide concrete tools for analyzing change, approximating functions, finding extreme values, solving dynamic problems, and understanding the shape of graphs. The core applications include:

- **Rates of change** (instantaneous velocity, acceleration)
- **Tangent and normal lines** to curves
- **Linear approximations and differentials**
- **Optimization** (max/min problems)
- **Related rates** (time‑dependent relationships)
- **Curve sketching** (monotonicity, concavity, inflection points)
- **Mean Value Theorem** and its theoretical consequences

Mastery of these topics allows a student to model and solve a vast array of practical and theoretical problems in mathematics, physics, engineering, economics, and beyond. The derivative truly lives up to its reputation as a fundamental pillar of calculus.
