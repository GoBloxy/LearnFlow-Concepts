# Research Methods and Statistics

## Introduction
Research methods and statistics form the backbone of psychological science. They provide the tools to ask meaningful questions, design rigorous studies, and draw valid conclusions about human thoughts, feelings, and behaviour. In this article, you will learn about the core principles of psychological research—from generating a hypothesis to analysing data and interpreting results. We will explore the major research designs, key statistical concepts, and ethical considerations, illustrating each with examples relevant to undergraduate psychology.

## The Scientific Method in Psychology
Psychology is an empirical science. The scientific method involves a systematic process:

1. **Observation** – noticing a phenomenon or pattern
2. **Formulating a research question** – turning the observation into a testable question
3. **Developing a hypothesis** – a specific, falsifiable prediction
4. **Designing and conducting a study** – gathering data to test the hypothesis
5. **Analysing data** – using statistical techniques
6. **Drawing conclusions** – interpreting findings, considering limitations, and refining theory

For example, a psychologist might notice that students who sit at the front of a lecture hall seem to perform better. This could lead to the hypothesis: “Students sitting in the front row will have significantly higher exam scores than those sitting in the back row.”

## Core Concepts in Research

### Variables and Measurement
A **variable** is any characteristic that can take on different values. In psychology, we distinguish between:

- **Independent variable (IV)** – the factor manipulated by the researcher.
- **Dependent variable (DV)** – the outcome measured.
- **Confounding variable** – an extraneous factor that could influence the DV and distort the relationship.

Variables can be measured at different **scales of measurement**:
- **Nominal** – categories with no order (e.g., eye colour)
- **Ordinal** – ordered categories (e.g., class rank)
- **Interval** – equal intervals but no true zero (e.g., temperature in Celsius)
- **Ratio** – equal intervals with a meaningful zero (e.g., reaction time in milliseconds)

### Reliability and Validity
- **Reliability** – consistency of a measure. A reliable intelligence test yields similar scores when retaken under similar conditions.
- **Validity** – the measure truly captures what it claims to measure. A test of depression must actually assess depressive symptoms, not anxiety.

### Populations and Samples
- **Population** – the entire group of interest (e.g., all UK undergraduates).
- **Sample** – a subset drawn from the population, which should be **representative** to allow generalisation.
- **Random sampling** is ideal, though often convenience or stratified sampling is used.

## Research Designs

### Experimental Methods
An experiment involves manipulating the IV while controlling other variables and measuring the effect on the DV. The key feature is **random assignment** of participants to conditions, which helps establish cause–effect relationships.

**Example:** To test if caffeine improves memory, you assign 60 participants randomly to a caffeine group (IV: 200 mg pill) or a placebo group. Then you measure the number of words recalled from a list (DV). Random assignment balances individual differences, so any difference in recall can be attributed causally to caffeine.

### Correlational Research
When variables cannot be manipulated (e.g., personality traits, age), correlational designs examine the relationship between two measured variables. No cause–effect conclusion is warranted because of possible **third variables**.

**Example:** You measure hours of sleep per night and GPA. A positive correlation ($r > 0$) might emerge, but you cannot say sleep *causes* higher grades. Perhaps conscientiousness causes both.

### Observational and Qualitative Designs
- **Naturalistic observation** – observing behaviour in its natural setting without interference (e.g., playground aggression).
- **Surveys and interviews** – self-report methods for attitudes, beliefs, or experiences.
- **Case studies** – in-depth analysis of a single individual or small group, often used in clinical psychology.

## Descriptive Statistics
Descriptive statistics summarise and organise data from a sample.

### Measures of Central Tendency
- **Mean** ($M$): arithmetic average  
  $M = \frac{\sum X}{N}$
- **Median**: the middle score when ordered.
- **Mode**: the most frequent score.

**Example:** Reaction times (ms): 250, 270, 280, 300, 320.  
$M = \frac{250+270+280+300+320}{5} = 284$ ms. Median = 280 ms. Mode: none (all unique).

### Measures of Dispersion
- **Range**: maximum – minimum.
- **Variance** ($s^2$): average squared deviation from the mean.  
  For a sample:  
  $s^2 = \frac{\sum (X - M)^2}{N-1}$
- **Standard deviation** ($SD$): square root of variance, expressing variability in original units.  
  $SD = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (X - M)^2}{N-1}}$

Using the reaction times above: $M=284$. Deviations: -34, -14, -4, 16, 36. Squared: 1156, 196, 16, 256, 1296. Sum = 2920. $s^2 = 2920 / 4 = 730$. $SD \approx 27.02$ ms.

### The Normal Distribution
Many psychological variables (e.g., IQ scores) follow a bell-shaped normal curve. In a normal distribution:
- 68% of scores fall within 1 SD of the mean.
- 95% within 2 SDs.
- 99.7% within 3 SDs.

## Inferential Statistics
Inferential statistics allow us to determine whether observed patterns are likely due to chance and to generalise from samples to populations.

### Hypothesis Testing
We evaluate two competing hypotheses:
- **Null hypothesis ($H_0$)** – no effect or no difference (e.g., caffeine does not influence memory).
- **Alternative hypothesis ($H_1$)** – the research prediction (e.g., caffeine affects memory).

A **p-value** tells us the probability of obtaining our observed results (or more extreme) if $H_0$ were true. If $p < \alpha$ (commonly $\alpha = 0.05$), we reject $H_0$ and declare the result **statistically significant**. A $p$-value of, say, 0.03 means that if the null were true, we would see such an effect only 3% of the time — unlikely enough under $H_0$ to favour $H_1$.

**Important:** Significance does not equal importance. A tiny effect can be significant with a large sample.

### Effect Size
Effect sizes quantify the magnitude of a relationship or difference, independent of sample size.
- **Cohen’s d**: difference between two means divided by pooled standard deviation.  
  $d = \frac{M_1 - M_2}{SD_{pooled}}$
- **Pearson’s r**: correlation coefficient (already an effect size).  
  Guidelines: $|d| \approx 0.2$ small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large; $|r| \approx 0.10$ small, 0.30 medium, 0.50 large.

### Common Statistical Tests

#### t-Test
Compares means of two groups.  
**Independent samples t-test** – for between-subjects designs (e.g., caffeine vs. placebo).  
$t = \frac{M_1 - M_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}$  
Degrees of freedom ($df$) ≈ $n_1 + n_2 - 2$. Compare computed $t$ to critical $t$ from a table (or use software) to obtain the $p$-value.

**Paired samples t-test** – for within-subjects (e.g., memory scores before vs. after caffeine in the same participants).

#### Correlation and Regression
**Pearson’s r** measures linear association:  
$r = \frac{\sum (X - \bar{X})(Y - \bar{Y})}{\sqrt{\sum (X - \bar{X})^2 \sum (Y - \bar{Y})^2}}$  
$r$ ranges from –1 to +1. Testing $r$ for significance uses a $t$-test with $df = n-2$.

**Linear regression** predicts $Y$ from $X$:  
$\hat{Y} = a + bX$  
Where $b$ = slope (change in $Y$ per unit $X$), $a$ = intercept. $R^2$ indicates proportion of variance explained.

#### ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)
When comparing three or more groups, ANOVA tests whether any group means differ. The **F-ratio** is  
$F = \frac{\text{variance between groups}}{\text{variance within groups}}$  
A significant $F$ is followed by *post-hoc* tests (e.g., Tukey’s HSD) to locate the differences.

### Confidence Intervals
A 95% confidence interval for a mean difference tells us a range of plausible values for the true population difference. If the interval for the difference between two means excludes zero, the difference is significant at $\alpha = 0.05$. Confidence intervals convey precision: narrow intervals indicate more reliable estimates.

## Ethical Considerations
Psychology researchers must adhere to ethical guidelines (e.g., BPS or APA codes):
- **Informed consent** – participants understand the study and voluntarily agree.
- **Right to withdraw** – participants can leave at any time without penalty.
- **Deception** – must be justified scientifically, with thorough debriefing.
- **Confidentiality** – data are anonymised and securely stored.
- **Protection from harm** – physical and psychological wellbeing is paramount.

An ethics committee must approve all research before it begins.

## Summary
- Research methods in psychology range from rigorous experiments that can establish causality to correlational and observational designs that explore relationships where manipulation is impossible.
- The quality of research depends on reliable and valid measurement, careful control of confounds, and ethical practice.
- Descriptive statistics (mean, standard deviation) summarize data; inferential statistics (t-tests, ANOVA, correlation) test hypotheses about populations, with $p$-values and effect sizes guiding interpretation.
- A critical understanding of these methods allows you to evaluate psychological claims and design your own studies, ultimately advancing knowledge about human behaviour.
