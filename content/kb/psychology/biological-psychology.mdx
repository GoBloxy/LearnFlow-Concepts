# Biological Psychology

## Introduction
Biological psychology, also called biopsychology or psychobiology, is the scientific study of the biological substrates of behavior and mental processes. It bridges the disciplines of psychology and biology, seeking to understand how the brain, nervous system, hormones, and genes give rise to our thoughts, emotions, and actions. At the undergraduate level, the field explores how neural activity translates into perception, memory, motivation, and social interaction, emphasizing that all psychological phenomena are also biological events.

The roots of biological psychology lie in ancient philosophical debates about the mind–body relationship, but it became an empirical science with the discovery of nerve cells by Santiago Ramón y Cajal, the neuron doctrine, and the subsequent development of neuroimaging. Today, the field integrates cellular and molecular neuroscience, behavioral genetics, endocrinology, and cognitive science to form a comprehensive picture of the living organism.

## Neurons and Neural Communication

### Structure of a Neuron
The fundamental unit of the nervous system is the neuron. A typical neuron consists of:
- **Dendrites**: branched fibers that receive signals from other neurons.
- **Soma (cell body)**: contains the nucleus and metabolic machinery.
- **Axon hillock**: the junction between soma and axon where action potentials are initiated.
- **Axon**: a long fiber that conducts electrical impulses away from the soma.
- **Axon terminals (boutons)**: bulbous endings that form synapses with target cells.

Many axons are insulated by a myelin sheath formed by glial cells (oligodendrocytes in the central nervous system, Schwann cells in the periphery), which greatly increases conduction speed.

### The Resting Membrane Potential
Neurons maintain an electrical potential difference across their membrane when at rest, typically around $-70$ mV (inside negative relative to outside). This resting potential arises primarily from unequal distributions of ions, particularly potassium ($K^+$), sodium ($Na^+$), chloride ($Cl^-$), and large intracellular anions ($A^-$), combined with selective permeability of the membrane.

The equilibrium potential for a single ion can be calculated using the **Nernst equation**:

\[
E_{ion} = \frac{RT}{zF} \ln\frac{[ion]_{out}}{[ion]_{in}}
\]

where:
- $E_{ion}$ = equilibrium potential (V)
- $R$ = universal gas constant ($8.314\ \text{J·mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$)
- $T$ = absolute temperature (K)
- $z$ = valence of the ion
- $F$ = Faraday constant ($96,485\ \text{C·mol}^{-1}$)
- $[ion]_{out}$, $[ion]_{in}$ = extracellular and intracellular concentrations

At body temperature ($37^\circ$C), converting to $\log_{10}$ and expressing in mV, the Nernst equation simplifies to:

\[
E_{ion} = \frac{61.5\ \text{mV}}{z} \log_{10}\frac{[ion]_{out}}{[ion]_{in}}
\]

For $K^+$ with typical concentrations (extracellular $5\ \text{mM}$, intracellular $140\ \text{mM}$):

\[
E_{K^+} = \frac{61.5}{+1} \log_{10}\frac{5}{140} \approx 61.5 \times (-1.45) \approx -89\ \text{mV}
\]

The resting membrane potential is more positive than $E_{K^+}$ because the membrane is slightly permeable to $Na^+$. The **Goldman–Hodgkin–Katz (GHK) equation** accounts for multiple permeant ions:

\[
V_m = \frac{RT}{F} \ln\frac{P_{K}[K^+]_{out} + P_{Na}[Na^+]_{out} + P_{Cl}[Cl^-]_{in}}{P_{K}[K^+]_{in} + P_{Na}[Na^+]_{in} + P_{Cl}[Cl^-]_{out}}
\]

where $P_{ion}$ represents the relative membrane permeability for each ion. The resting $V_m$ of approximately $-70$ mV is a weighted average of these equilibrium potentials.

### The Action Potential
An action potential is a rapid, transient excursion of the membrane potential that serves as the primary electrical signal in neurons. It is triggered when the membrane potential at the axon hillock reaches a threshold (around $-55$ mV). The process involves voltage-gated ion channels:
1. **Depolarization**: Voltage-gated $Na^+$ channels open, $Na^+$ rushes inward, driving the membrane potential toward $E_{Na^+}$ ($\approx +60$ mV).
2. **Repolarization**: $Na^+$ channels inactivate, voltage-gated $K^+$ channels open, $K^+$ exits, restoring negative polarity.
3. **Hyperpolarization**: $K^+$ channels close slowly, the membrane briefly becomes more negative than resting.
4. The $Na^+/K^+$ ATPase pump restores the original ion gradients.

Action potentials are all-or-none and propagate without decrement along myelinated axons via saltatory conduction, jumping from one node of Ranvier to the next.

*Example*: In a sensory neuron responding to touch, a mechanical stimulus opens ion channels, producing a graded receptor potential. If the depolarization reaches threshold, a train of action potentials is generated, the frequency of which encodes stimulus intensity.

### Synaptic Transmission
Information flows from one neuron to the next at synapses. Most synapses in the mammalian nervous system are chemical:
- An action potential arrives at the presynaptic terminal.
- Depolarization opens voltage-gated $Ca^{2+}$ channels; $Ca^{2+}$ influx triggers vesicles filled with neurotransmitter to fuse with the membrane and release their contents into the synaptic cleft.
- Neurotransmitter molecules diffuse and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane.
- Binding opens (or closes) ion channels, producing an excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP) or inhibitory postsynaptic potential (IPSP).

The integration of many EPSPs and IPSPs across the dendrites and soma determines whether the postsynaptic neuron reaches threshold. Neurotransmitter action is terminated by reuptake, enzymatic degradation, or diffusion.

## The Nervous System
The nervous system is divided into the **central nervous system** (CNS: brain and spinal cord) and the **peripheral nervous system** (PNS: nerves and ganglia outside the CNS).

### Central Nervous System
The brain can be organized by major divisions:
- **Hindbrain**: medulla oblongata (vital reflexes such as breathing, heart rate), pons (sleep, arousal relaying), cerebellum (motor coordination, balance, procedural learning).
- **Midbrain**: substantia nigra (dopamine production for movement), superior and inferior colliculi (visual and auditory reflexes).
- **Forebrain**: thalamus (sensory relay station), hypothalamus (homeostasis, hormone regulation, drives), limbic system (emotion, memory: amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate gyrus), basal ganglia (movement control), and the cerebral cortex.

The **cerebral cortex** is the convoluted outer layer, responsible for higher-order functions. It is divided into four lobes:
- **Frontal lobe**: executive functions, planning, working memory, motor control (primary motor cortex in precentral gyrus).
- **Parietal lobe**: somatosensory processing (postcentral gyrus), spatial attention.
- **Temporal lobe**: auditory processing, memory formation (hippocampus located medially), language comprehension (Wernicke’s area).
- **Occipital lobe**: vision.

*Example*: Damage to Broca’s area in the left frontal lobe results in non‑fluent aphasia, where speech production is labored but comprehension remains relatively intact, illustrating the lateralization and localization of language functions.

### Peripheral Nervous System
The PNS comprises:
- **Somatic nervous system**: controls voluntary movements via motor neurons that innervate skeletal muscles; transmits sensory information from skin, muscles, and joints.
- **Autonomic nervous system**: regulates involuntary bodily functions. It has two branches:
  - **Sympathetic division**: mobilizes the body during stress (“fight or flight”); increases heart rate, dilates pupils, inhibits digestion.
  - **Parasympathetic division**: promotes “rest and digest” activities; slows heart rate, stimulates digestion.

*Example*: When encountering a threat, sympathetic activation releases norepinephrine at target organs and epinephrine from the adrenal medulla, raising blood pressure and redirecting blood flow to muscles, preparing the body for rapid action.

## Neurotransmitters and Behavior
Neurotransmitters are the chemical messengers that mediate communication between neurons. Their effects depend on the receptor type and the neural circuit involved. Key neurotransmitters include:

- **Dopamine (DA)**: Involved in reward, motivation, motor control, and executive function. The mesolimbic dopamine pathway, projecting from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens, is central to the experience of pleasure and reinforcement. Degeneration of the nigrostriatal dopamine pathway causes Parkinson’s disease (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia), while dysregulation of dopamine is implicated in schizophrenia and addiction.
- **Serotonin (5-HT)**: Regulates mood, appetite, sleep, and pain. Many antidepressants (SSRIs) increase serotonergic signaling by blocking reuptake, consistent with the monoamine hypothesis of depression.
- **Acetylcholine (ACh)**: Acts at neuromuscular junctions to trigger muscle contraction; in the CNS, it is important for attention, learning, and memory. Loss of cholinergic neurons in the basal forebrain is associated with Alzheimer’s disease.
- **GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)**: The primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain. It reduces neuronal excitability. Benzodiazepines and alcohol enhance GABA_A receptor function, producing anxiolytic and sedative effects.
- **Glutamate**: The main excitatory CNS neurotransmitter, critical for synaptic plasticity, learning, and memory. Excessive glutamate signaling can cause excitotoxicity, contributing to neuronal damage after stroke.

*Example*: The rewarding effects of drugs of abuse often converge on the dopamine system. Cocaine blocks the reuptake of dopamine from the synapse, causing an accumulation that intensifies and prolongs signaling in the nucleus accumbens, leading to the subjective “high” and reinforcing drug-seeking behavior.

## Hormones and Behavior
Hormones are chemical signals secreted by endocrine glands into the bloodstream, affecting organ function and behavior. Unlike fast synaptic transmission, hormonal effects are slower and more sustained.

The **hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal (HPA) axis** is a central stress response system:
- The hypothalamus releases corticotropin‑releasing hormone (CRH).
- CRH stimulates the anterior pituitary to secrete adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH).
- ACTH prompts the adrenal cortex to release **cortisol** (a glucocorticoid).
- Cortisol raises blood glucose, suppresses immune function, and facilitates the stress response. Chronic activation can impair hippocampal function, contributing to memory deficits and mood disorders.

Other important hormones:
- **Oxytocin**: Synthesized in the hypothalamus and released by the posterior pituitary; facilitates social bonding, maternal behavior, and trust. It is sometimes called the “love hormone.”
- **Sex steroids** (testosterone, estradiol): Influence sexual differentiation of the brain, reproductive behavior, and aggression. In rodents, a surge of testosterone during a critical perinatal period masculinizes the brain, while in adulthood these hormones modulate libido and partly parental behavior.
- **Melatonin**: Produced by the pineal gland, it regulates circadian rhythms and is released during darkness, promoting sleep.

*Example*: In the classic “tend-and-befriend” model, oxytocin secreted during stress may counteract fight‑or‑flight responses, particularly in women, by encouraging social affiliation and caregiving behaviors.

## Genetics and Behavior
Behavioral genetics examines how genes contribute to individual differences in behavior. While no single gene determines a complex psychological trait, multiple genes interact with the environment to influence liability.

### Heritability
Heritability ($h^2$) estimates the proportion of phenotypic variance in a population attributable to genetic variance. It is not a fixed property of a trait but depends on the range of environments and genetic variation. Twin and adoption studies are used to estimate $h^2$: if identical (monozygotic, MZ) twins are more similar on a trait than fraternal (dizygotic, DZ) twins, genetic factors are implicated.

*Example*: Schizophrenia has a heritability of about 80%. The concordance rate (probability that both twins have the disorder) is roughly 45–50% for MZ twins versus 10–15% for DZ twins. This strong genetic component is coupled with environmental risk factors such as prenatal infection or urban upbringing.

### Epigenetics
Epigenetics refers to changes in gene expression that do not involve alterations to the DNA sequence. Environmental experiences can attach methyl groups to DNA or modify histones, silencing or activating genes. Early life stress, for instance, can alter the methylation of the glucocorticoid receptor gene, affecting an individual’s stress reactivity across the lifespan.

## Methods in Biological Psychology
Biopsychologists use a variety of techniques to link brain and behavior.

- **Electroencephalography (EEG)**: Records electrical activity from the scalp with millisecond precision. Used to study sleep stages, evoked potentials, and epilepsy.
- **Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) and functional MRI (fMRI)**: MRI provides high‑resolution structural images. fMRI measures blood‑oxygen‑level‑dependent (BOLD) signals, indirectly mapping neural activity during cognitive tasks.
- **Positron Emission Tomography (PET)**: Detects radioactive tracers to visualize metabolic activity or receptor binding. Useful for studying neurotransmitter systems.
- **Lesion and stimulation studies**: Animal models and human case studies (e.g., Phineas Gage, patient H.M.) reveal the function of brain regions. Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) can temporarily disrupt or enhance cortical activity to test causal hypotheses.
- **Single‑cell recording**: Inserting microelectrodes into brain tissue records action potentials from individual neurons, allowing researchers to correlate firing patterns with specific stimuli or behaviors.

*Example*: In a typical fMRI experiment on face perception, researchers find that the fusiform face area (FFA) in the temporal lobe shows greater activation when participants view faces compared to objects, demonstrating functional specialization in the visual system.

## Biological Rhythms and Consciousness
Many behaviors follow biological clocks. **Circadian rhythms** are approximately 24‑hour cycles controlled by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus. The SCN receives light input from the retina, synchronizing internal clocks with the day–night cycle.

Sleep itself is a highly organized state measured with EEG:
- **Non‑REM sleep** progresses from light (stage N1) to deep slow‑wave sleep (stage N3), characterized by delta waves. Deep sleep is crucial for body restoration and memory consolidation.
- **REM sleep** features rapid eye movements, a desynchronized EEG similar to waking, and muscle atonia. Most dreaming occurs during REM, and it is thought to play a role in emotional regulation and synaptic plasticity.

Disruptions to circadian rhythms and sleep (shift work, jet lag, sleep disorders) can impair cognitive performance, mood, and metabolic health.

## Summary
Biological psychology provides the essential foundation for understanding behavior as a product of physical processes. Starting with the electrochemical signaling of individual neurons, the field scales up to circuits, brain systems, hormones, and genes. The resting membrane potential is described by the Nernst and GHK equations, action potentials propagate neural information, and synaptic transmission converts electrical signals into chemical messages. The nervous system’s organization—from the autonomic branches to the cortical lobes—underpins specific perceptual, motor, and cognitive functions.

Neurotransmitters like dopamine, serotonin, and GABA, as well as hormones such as cortisol, shape mood, motivation, and stress responses. Genetics and epigenetics explain how heritable predispositions and environmental influences combine to produce behavioral variation, while modern imaging and electrophysiological methods allow direct investigation of the living brain.
