# Cognitive Psychology

## Introduction

Cognitive psychology is the branch of psychology that studies mental processes, including how people perceive, think, remember, learn, speak, and solve problems. It emerged in the mid-20th century as a response to the limitations of behaviorism, shifting the focus from observable behavior to the inner workings of the mind. Often called the “science of the mind,” cognitive psychology uses rigorous experimental methods to infer what happens between stimulus and response. The field draws on an information-processing metaphor, comparing the mind to a computer that encodes, stores, and retrieves data. This article explores the foundational concepts of cognitive psychology—perception, attention, memory, language, and decision-making—with concrete examples and, where useful, mathematical notation.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Perception: Constructing Reality

Perception is the process by which sensory input is organized and interpreted. It is not a passive recording of the world but an active construction influenced by expectations, context, and prior knowledge.

- **Bottom-up processing** relies purely on incoming sensory data.
- **Top-down processing** uses higher-level knowledge to shape perception.

**Example**: Consider the ambiguous “Rat–Man” drawing (the “ambiguous figure” illusion). Without context, the image may be seen as either a rat or a man. Once you are told “here is a man,” top-down processing guides perception toward the man’s face.

- **Gestalt principles** describe how we naturally group elements. For instance, the law of proximity states that objects close together are perceived as a group. The law of closure explains why we see a complete circle even when parts are missing.

**Mathematical note**: Signal detection theory models perception as a decision under uncertainty. A simple version can be expressed as:
\[
d' = \lambda(S_2) - \lambda(S_1)
\]
where $d'$ is the sensitivity index measuring the separation between signal and noise distributions, and $\lambda$ represents the standardized means of the sensory evidence under signal ($S_2$) and noise ($S_1$) conditions.

### 2. Attention: Selecting from the World

Attention is the cognitive mechanism that allows us to focus on some stimuli while ignoring others. Without attention, the rich world of sensation would overwhelm the mind.

- **Selective attention** is famously demonstrated by the **cocktail party effect**: you can attend to a single conversation in a noisy room while filtering out other voices. 
- **Dichotic listening experiments** (Cherry, 1953) show that unattended messages are rarely processed for meaning but physical changes (e.g., a human voice changing to a tone) are noticed, supporting early-selection models like Broadbent’s filter model.
- **Divided attention** reveals the limits of cognitive resources. The dual-task paradigm shows that performance suffers when two tasks demand the same resources. An everyday example is driving while talking on a hands-free phone—reaction times slow because of a shared central bottleneck.
- **Feature Integration Theory** (Treisman & Gelade, 1980) explains how we search for objects. A visual search for a red X among green Os is fast (pop-out) because it involves a single feature. Searching for a red X among red Os and green Xs is slow because attention must bind color and shape serially.

### 3. Memory: Encoding, Storage, and Retrieval

Memory is not a single entity but a collection of systems that allow us to retain information over time.

#### 3.1 The Modal Model

The Atkinson-Shiffrin model (1968) posits three stores:

- **Sensory memory**: brief retention of sensory information (iconic memory < 1 sec; echoic memory ~3 sec).
- **Short-term memory (STM)**: limited capacity (7 ± 2 chunks) and duration (~20–30 sec without rehearsal).
- **Long-term memory (LTM)**: vast, relatively permanent storage.

**Example**: Remembering a new phone number. You look at it (sensory), repeat it (STM), and eventually store it (LTM).

#### 3.2 Working Memory

Baddeley & Hitch (1974) replaced STM with a multi-component working memory system:

- **Phonological loop**: holds verbal/auditory information.
- **Visuospatial sketchpad**: holds visual and spatial information.
- **Central executive**: controls attention and coordinates the subsystems.
- **Episodic buffer**: integrates information across modalities (added later).

**Example**: Mental arithmetic. When calculating $23 \times 7$ in your head, the phonological loop rehearses the numbers, the visuospatial sketchpad may visualize the problem, and the central executive manages the steps.

#### 3.3 Long-Term Memory

Long-term memory divides into:

- **Explicit (declarative) memory**: 
  - *Semantic memory*: facts and general knowledge (e.g., Paris is the capital of France).
  - *Episodic memory*: personal experiences (e.g., your first day at university).
- **Implicit (non-declarative) memory**: 
  - *Procedural memory*: skills and habits (e.g., riding a bike).
  - *Priming*: exposure to a stimulus influences response to a later stimulus.

**Forgetting** is often described by Ebbinghaus’s forgetting curve, which can be approximated by a power law or exponential decay. A simple exponential model is:
\[
R = e^{-t / S}
\]
where $R$ is the retention proportion, $t$ is time, and $S$ represents the strength (durability) of the memory. The curve shows rapid initial forgetting that levels off over time.

### 4. Language: Structure and Use

Language is a system of symbols and rules that allows for the infinite generation of meaning. Cognitive psychology studies how language is acquired, understood, and produced.

- **Phonology**: the sounds of a language.
- **Morphology**: how words are formed from morphemes (smallest meaning units). For example, “un-likely” has three morphemes.
- **Syntax**: rules for sentence structure. The famous sentence “Colorless green ideas sleep furiously” (Chomsky) is syntactically correct but semantically anomalous.
- **Semantics**: meaning.
- **Pragmatics**: language use in context.

**The N400 effect**: an ERP component that appears about 400 ms after a semantically unexpected word (e.g., “He spread his toast with socks”) indexes semantic processing.

**Example of ambiguity**: “The old man the boat.” This sentence is initially misinterpreted because “old man” is a garden path; “man” is a verb meaning “to operate.” The reader must reparse the syntax, illustrating real-time sentence processing difficulty.

### 5. Thinking, Problem-Solving, and Decision-Making

Higher cognition involves manipulating mental representations to reach goals.

#### 5.1 Problem-Solving

- **Well-defined problems** have clear initial states, goal states, and operators (e.g., the Tower of Hanoi). Strategies include algorithms (systematic procedures) and heuristics (mental shortcuts).
- **Heuristics**: the means–end analysis involves breaking a problem into subgoals. The analogy approach uses a solution from a similar problem. **Mental set** (fixation) can hinder problem-solving; the water-jug problems demonstrate how past strategies can block more efficient solutions.
- **Insight problems** often require a restructuring of the problem representation. The “9-dot problem” (draw 4 straight lines connecting all 9 dots) requires thinking outside the perceived square.

#### 5.2 Judgment and Decision-Making

People often rely on heuristics that lead to systematic biases.

- **Availability heuristic**: judging frequency by the ease with which examples come to mind. After seeing news of a plane crash, people overestimate the danger of flying.
- **Representativeness heuristic**: judging probability based on similarity to a prototype. In Kahneman and Tversky’s “Linda problem,” participants incorrectly rate the probability of a conjunction (“bank teller and feminist”) higher than that of a single event (“bank teller”), ignoring the conjunction rule:
  \[
  P(A \cap B) \leq P(A)
  \]
- **Anchoring and adjustment**: estimates are influenced by an initial anchor. Asking whether the percentage of African nations in the UN is higher or lower than 10% leads to lower estimates than starting with 65%.
- **Prospect theory** (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) describes how people make choices involving risk, valuing gains and losses relative to a reference point and showing loss aversion. The value function is typically concave for gains and convex for losses, steeper for losses:
  \[
  v(x) = \begin{cases} 
   x^\alpha & \text{if } x \geq 0 \\
   -\lambda (-x)^\beta & \text{if } x < 0 
\end{cases}
\]
  with $\lambda > 1$ capturing loss aversion (typically $\lambda \approx 2.25$), $\alpha, \beta \lesssim 1$ representing diminishing sensitivity.

**Bayesian reasoning** is another normative model. In medical diagnosis, the probability of a disease given a positive test result depends on the base rate:
\[
P(\text{Disease} \mid +) = \frac{P(+ \mid \text{Disease}) \cdot P(\text{Disease})}{P(+)}
\]
People often neglect the base rate and overestimate the posterior probability, a cognitive bias known as base-rate neglect.

## Summary

Cognitive psychology provides a detailed map of the human mind, explaining how we perceive the world, attend to relevant stimuli, remember experiences, comprehend and produce language, and make decisions. Core themes include:

- The mind as an active processor, not a passive recorder.
- Limited capacity in attention and working memory, leading to selective processing and bottlenecks.
- The dependence of memory on encoding depth, retrieval cues, and forgetting over time.
- The role of heuristics and biases in judgment, which can deviate from normative rationality.

Understanding these processes not only illuminates everyday experience but also informs applications in education, clinical psychology, human–computer interaction, and artificial intelligence. Cognitive psychology continues to evolve through behavioral experiments, computational modeling, and neuroimaging, offering ever deeper insights into the architecture of cognition.
