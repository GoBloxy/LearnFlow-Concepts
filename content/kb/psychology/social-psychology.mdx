# Social Psychology

## Introduction

Social psychology is the scientific study of how people’s thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by the actual, imagined, or implied presence of others. It bridges psychology and sociology, exploring the interplay between individual minds and social contexts. Unlike personality psychology, which focuses on stable individual differences, or sociology, which examines group-level structures, social psychology examines how ordinary people construe their social world and how those construals shape everyday interactions.

Key themes include the power of the situation, the role of cognitive processes in social judgment, and the fundamental human need to belong. Research methods range from laboratory experiments to field studies, emphasizing causal inference and ecological validity. The discipline’s findings apply to domains such as prejudice, conformity, aggression, attraction, and group decision-making.

In this article, we will explore core concepts of social psychology, each illustrated with concrete examples. Topics include social cognition, attribution, attitudes and persuasion, social influence, group dynamics, prejudice and stereotyping, aggression, and prosocial behavior.

## Core Concepts

### Social Cognition

Social cognition examines how people perceive, remember, and interpret information about themselves and others. It rests on the idea that humans are cognitive misers: we conserve mental resources by using mental shortcuts (heuristics) and simplifying complex social information.

#### Schemas and Stereotypes
Schemas are organized knowledge structures that guide information processing. When encountering a new person, we activate relevant schemas (e.g., “professor,” “artist”) and fill in missing details. While efficient, schemas can lead to stereotyping—overgeneralized beliefs about a group. For example, believing that all elderly people are frail can cause one to misinterpret a healthy older adult’s capabilities.

#### Heuristics
- **Representativeness heuristic**: Judging the likelihood that someone belongs to a category based on how similar they are to a typical member. Example: Assuming a quiet, poetry-loving person is more likely to be an English major than a business major, even if business majors are far more numerous.
- **Availability heuristic**: Estimating frequency based on how easily examples come to mind. After seeing news reports of plane crashes, people may overestimate the danger of flying relative to driving.
- **Anchoring and adjustment**: Using a starting point as a reference and adjusting insufficiently. A real estate agent’s initial high listing price can anchor a buyer’s perception, making subsequent lower offers seem like bargains even if they remain above market value.

#### Attribution: Explaining Behavior
Attribution theory concerns how people explain the causes of events and behaviors. Fritz Heider distinguished between internal (personal) and external (situational) attributions.

- **Covariation model (Kelley)** : We attribute behavior based on consensus, distinctiveness, and consistency. If your friend raves about a film (high consistency), but others dislike it (low consensus) and she raves about many films (low distinctiveness), you attribute her enthusiasm to her disposition.
- **Fundamental attribution error**: Overestimating personality traits and underestimating situational forces when explaining others’ behavior. If someone cuts us off in traffic, we assume they are rude rather than that they might be rushing to a hospital. This error is partly due to perceptual salience—the actor is salient, not the situation.
- **Self-serving bias**: Taking credit for successes (internal attribution) while blaming failures on external factors. A student attributes an A to their intelligence but a D to an unfair exam.

### Attitudes and Persuasion

An attitude is a positive, negative, or mixed evaluation of an object, person, or idea. Attitudes consist of affective (emotional), behavioral, and cognitive components. They predict behavior when they are strong, specific, and accessible.

#### Attitude Formation and Measurement
Attitudes can be formed through direct experience, classical conditioning (e.g., pairing a product with a pleasant song), operant conditioning (rewarding certain expressions), and observational learning. Self-report measures (Likert scales) are common, though implicit association tests (IAT) tap automatic, unconscious attitudes.

#### Cognitive Dissonance Theory
Leon Festinger proposed that inconsistencies between attitudes and behavior create psychological discomfort (dissonance), motivating people to reduce it. For instance, after choosing between two equally attractive job offers, people enhance the chosen and devalue the rejected alternative to justify their decision. In a classic experiment, participants paid only $1 to lie about a boring task reported enjoying it more than those paid $20, because the $1 group lacked sufficient external justification and thus changed their attitude to reduce dissonance.

#### Persuasion
The Yale attitude change approach examines who says what to whom. Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo) distinguishes two routes:

- **Central route**: Systematic processing of arguments when motivation and ability are high. Leads to strong, enduring attitude change. Example: A voter carefully studies candidates’ policy positions.
- **Peripheral route**: Relies on superficial cues (source attractiveness, number of arguments) when elaboration is low. Example: Buying a product endorsed by a celebrity without scrutinizing its merits.

Resistance to persuasion increases with forewarning, reactance (reacting against perceived threat to freedom), and prior public commitment.

### Social Influence

Social influence encompasses the ways people affect one another’s attitudes and behaviors. Major forms include conformity, compliance, and obedience.

#### Conformity
Conformity is a change in behavior or belief to align with a group norm. Solomon Asch’s line-judgment experiments showed that a substantial minority of participants conformed to an obviously incorrect majority judgment at least once. Drivers of conformity: normative influence (desire to be liked) and informational influence (desire to be correct). Factors increasing conformity: group size (up to about 4–5), unanimity, cohesion, and cultural values (collectivistic cultures tend to show higher conformity on the surface).

#### Compliance
Compliance refers to changing behavior in response to a direct request. Cialdini’s principles include:
- **Reciprocity**: Feeling obligated to return a favor. Free samples in stores.
- **Commitment/Consistency**: Once people commit to a small request, they are more likely to agree to a larger one (foot-in-the-door technique). Example: Signing a petition leads to displaying a large lawn sign.
- **Social proof**: Following what others are doing. “Best-selling” labels.
- **Authority**: Obeying experts or authority figures.
- **Liking**: Saying yes to people we like.
- **Scarcity**: Valuing limited opportunities.

#### Obedience
Stanley Milgram’s controversial experiments tested obedience to authority. Ordinary people administered what they believed were dangerous electric shocks to a learner when instructed by an experimenter. About 65% went to the maximum voltage. The study revealed that situational forces (proximity of authority, legitimacy of institution, gradual escalation) can override moral objections. Debates continue about ethical boundaries and the power of the situation.

### Group Processes

Groups influence individual behavior through social facilitation, social loafing, deindividuation, group polarization, and groupthink.

#### Social Facilitation and Social Loafing
- **Social facilitation**: The presence of others enhances performance on well-practiced or simple tasks (dominant response) but impairs performance on complex, novel tasks. A marathon runner runs faster with spectators; a new speech giver falters before an audience.
- **Social loafing**: People exert less effort when working collectively than when individually accountable. Tug-of-war teams pull less per person than they would alone. Reduced by identifiability, task importance, and group cohesion.

#### Deindividuation
In anonymous, arousing group settings, people may lose self-awareness and restraint, leading to impulsive, deviant behavior. Examples include riots or online trolling where identity is obscured.

#### Group Polarization and Groupthink
- **Group polarization**: Group discussion tends to intensify initial leanings. A jury slightly inclined toward conviction becomes more certain after deliberation.
- **Groupthink**: In highly cohesive, insulated groups, the desire for consensus overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives (Janis). Symptoms include illusions of invulnerability, collective rationalization, and pressure on dissenters. Historical example: The Bay of Pigs invasion decision.

### Prejudice, Stereotyping, and Discrimination

Prejudice is a negative attitude toward a group and its members; stereotypes are cognitive beliefs; discrimination is negative behavior. These intergroup phenomena arise from multiple roots.

#### Sources of Prejudice
- **Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner)** : People derive self-esteem from group memberships, leading to in-group favoritism and out-group derogation. Minimal group experiments show that even arbitrary, trivial groups trigger bias.
- **Realistic conflict theory**: Competition for limited resources breeds hostility. Muzafer Sherif’s Robbers Cave experiment showed that intergroup competition escalated conflict, while superordinate goals reduced it.
- **Cognitive processes**: Categorization automatically emphasizes within-group similarities and between-group differences; the out-group homogeneity effect perceives out-group members as “all alike.”

#### Implicit Bias
Implicit biases are unconscious associations that can influence judgments and behavior. The Implicit Association Test (IAT) reveals that most people hold automatic preferences for socially advantaged groups, even if they explicitly endorse egalitarian views. These biases can affect hiring decisions, medical treatment, and policing.

#### Reducing Prejudice
Contact hypothesis (Allport) specifies that intergroup contact reduces prejudice when there is equal status, common goals, intergroup cooperation, and institutional support. Programs such as jigsaw classrooms, where diverse students must collaborate to learn, have been effective.

### Aggression

Aggression is behavior intended to harm another person who does not wish to be harmed. It can be physical or relational (e.g., spreading rumors, social exclusion).

#### Theoretical Perspectives
- **Biological**: Genetic predispositions, brain regions (amygdala, prefrontal cortex), and hormones (testosterone) influence aggression but interact strongly with environment.
- **Frustration–aggression hypothesis**: Frustration—the blocking of goal-directed behavior—elicits aggression. Displaced aggression occurs when the source of frustration is intangible or too powerful; a scolded worker may yell at their spouse.
- **Social learning theory (Bandura)** : Aggression is learned through observation and imitation. The Bobo doll studies demonstrated that children who watched an adult model hit a doll were more likely to do so, especially if the model was rewarded.

#### Situational Influences
Heat, pain, crowding, and alcohol can amplify aggressive impulses. Media violence provides scripts and desensitization, particularly when violence is portrayed as justified, rewarded, and without realistic consequences. Cyberbullying illustrates the disinhibiting effects of anonymity and lack of immediate feedback.

### Prosocial Behavior

Prosocial behavior is any act intended to benefit others. Altruism is a subset motivated purely by concern for the other’s welfare.

#### Why Do We Help?
- **Evolutionary perspectives**: Kin selection (helping relatives to pass on genes) and reciprocal altruism (helping with expectation of future return) explain some helping.
- **Social exchange theory**: Helping is rational when rewards (feeling good, social approval) outweigh costs (danger, time).
- **Empathy–altruism hypothesis (Batson)** : When we feel empathy for someone, we help for altruistic reasons, not just to reduce our own distress.

#### Bystander Effect
The tragic murder of Kitty Genovese spurred research on the bystander effect: the more people who witness an emergency, the less likely any one individual is to help. Reasons include diffusion of responsibility and pluralistic ignorance (looking to others’ calm reactions to define the situation as non-emergency). Knowing these mechanisms increases the likelihood of intervention—making a specific request (“You in the red shirt, call 911”) breaks the cycle.

#### Increasing Helping
- Reducing ambiguity: clearly label emergencies.
- Modeling prosocial behavior.
- Teaching moral inclusion: expanding one’s circle of moral concern.
- Implementing volunteering programs and community service.

### The Self in Social Context

The self is fundamentally social. Our self-concept is shaped by reflected appraisals (Cooley’s looking-glass self) and social comparisons. Social comparison theory (Festinger) suggests we evaluate our abilities and opinions by comparing ourselves to others, especially when objective standards are lacking. Upward comparisons can inspire or deflate; downward comparisons can boost self-esteem.

Self-presentation (impression management) is the process by which we try to control how others perceive us. Self-monitoring refers to individual differences in sensitivity to social cues and ability to adjust behavior accordingly. High self-monitors adapt to situations like social chameleons; low self-monitors behave consistently with their inner attitudes.

## Summary

Social psychology illuminates the profound influence of social contexts on human behavior. Through systematic methods, it has uncovered principles that often contradict intuitive assumptions, such as the subtle power of situational forces over personality, the automaticity of stereotypes, and the counterintuitive dynamics of helping and harming.

Key takeaways include:
- **Social cognition** relies on schemas and heuristics, which are efficient but error-prone; attribution biases like the fundamental attribution error shape our explanations of behavior.
- **Attitudes** guide behavior when strong and accessible; cognitive dissonance drives attitude change when behavior clashes with beliefs.
- **Social influence** operates through conformity, compliance, and obedience, demonstrating how mundane situational factors can override personal conscience.
- **Group dynamics** can amplify performance or lead to loafing, polarization, and flawed consensus.
- **Prejudice** stems from identity, conflict, and cognitive shortcuts, but can be reduced through structured intergroup contact.
- **Aggression** arises from frustration, learning, and situational triggers, while **prosocial behavior** is hindered by diffusion of responsibility but encouraged by empathy and clear responsibility.
- The **social self** is formed in relation to others, constantly navigating identity, comparison, and presentation.

Understanding these concepts equips us to recognize the hidden forces shaping everyday life and to design interventions that promote healthier, fairer, and more compassionate communities. Social psychology remains an essential lens for addressing societal challenges, from discrimination to public health to conflict resolution.
